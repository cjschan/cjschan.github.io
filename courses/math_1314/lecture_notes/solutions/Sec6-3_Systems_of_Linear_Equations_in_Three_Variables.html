Section 6.3

Systems of Linear Equations in Three Variables

Worked Solutions

A linear equation in three variables describes a plane. A system of such equations may have a unique point of intersection, a line of intersection, a plane, or no solution at all. We’ll solve systematically using elimination.

What Does a Solution Look Like?

Three Possibilities

For three planes:

  • Unique point: the three planes meet at a single point. Consistent and independent.
  • Line of intersection: infinitely many solutions parameterized by one variable.
  • No common point: planes parallel or pairwise meeting in three distinct lines. Inconsistent.

Solving by Elimination

Strategy
  1. Pick a variable (typically the one whose coefficients are simplest) and use pairs of equations to eliminate it, producing two equations in the remaining two variables.
  2. Solve the resulting 2 × 2 system using elimination or substitution.
  3. Back-substitute into one of the original equations to find the third variable.
  4. Check the answer in all three original equations.
Example 1

Unique solution

Solve.

\(\begin{cases} x + y + z = 6 \\ 2x - y + z = 3 \\ x + 2y - z = 2 \end{cases}\)

Eliminate \(z\) from the first two equations: subtract the first from the second to get \(x - 2y = -3\). Add the first and third to eliminate \(z\): \(2x + 3y = 8\).

From \(x - 2y = -3\): \(x = 2y - 3\). Substitute: \(2(2y - 3) + 3y = 8\), \(7y = 14\), \(y = 2\). Then \(x = 1\). Back-substitute into \(x + y + z = 6\): \(1 + 2 + z = 6\), \(z = 3\).

Solution: \((1, 2, 3)\).

Example 2

Inconsistent system

Solve.

\(\begin{cases} x + y - z = 2 \\ 2x + 2y - 2z = 7 \\ 3x + y + z = 4 \end{cases}\)

Multiply the first by 2: \(2x + 2y - 2z = 4\). Compare with the second: \(2x + 2y - 2z = 7\). Two contradictory statements — no solution.

Example 3

Dependent system

Solve.

\(\begin{cases} x + y + z = 4 \\ 2x - y + 3z = 5 \\ x - 2y + 2z = 1 \end{cases}\)

Eliminate \(x\): subtract the third from the first to get \(3y - z = 3\). Multiply the first by 2 and subtract the second: \(3y - z = 3\) again — same equation.

So we only have one independent relationship between \(y\) and \(z\). Let \(z = t\). Then \(y = \dfrac{3 + t}{3} = 1 + \dfrac{t}{3}\), and from the first equation \(x = 4 - y - z = 3 - \dfrac{4t}{3}\).

Solution: \(\left(3 - \tfrac{4t}{3},\; 1 + \tfrac{t}{3},\; t\right)\) for any real \(t\) — a line.

An Application

Example 4

Three-product mix

A bakery produces three kinds of cookies. Each batch of A uses 2 lb flour, 1 lb sugar, 1 lb butter. Each batch of B uses 3 lb flour, 1 lb sugar, 2 lb butter. Each batch of C uses 1 lb flour, 2 lb sugar, 1 lb butter. The bakery uses exactly 23 lb flour, 14 lb sugar, and 13 lb butter. How many batches of each were made?

Let \(a, b, c\) be the numbers of batches.

\(\begin{cases} 2a + 3b + c = 23 \\ a + b + 2c = 14 \\ a + 2b + c = 13 \end{cases}\)

Subtract the third from the first: \(a + b = 10\). Subtract the third from the second: \(-b + c = 1\), so \(c = b + 1\). Substitute into the second: \(a + b + 2(b + 1) = 14\), so \(a + 3b = 12\). Combine with \(a + b = 10\): \(2b = 2\), \(b = 1\). Then \(a = 9\), \(c = 2\).

9 batches of A, 1 batch of B, 2 batches of C.
