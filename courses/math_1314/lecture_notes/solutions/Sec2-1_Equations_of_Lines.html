Section 2.1

Equations of Lines

Worked Solutions

We turn the geometric picture of a line into algebra. We’ll see three forms of the equation of a line and use them to write equations through given points and to test whether two lines are parallel or perpendicular.

Slope-Intercept Form

Slope-Intercept Form

The slope-intercept form of a non-vertical line is \[ y = mx + b, \] where \(m\) is the slope and \((0, b)\) is the \(y\)-intercept.

Example 1

Reading slope and intercept

For each line, identify the slope and the \(y\)-intercept.

  1. \(y = 3x - 5\)
  2. \(y = -\tfrac{1}{2} x\)
  3. \(2x + 3y = 12\)

(a) Slope \(3\), intercept \((0, -5)\).

(b) Slope \(-\tfrac{1}{2}\), intercept \((0, 0)\).

(c) Solve for \(y\): \(3y = -2x + 12\), so \(y = -\tfrac{2}{3} x + 4\). Slope \(-\tfrac{2}{3}\), intercept \((0, 4)\).

Point-Slope Form

Point-Slope Form

The line with slope \(m\) passing through the point \((x_1, y_1)\) has equation \[ y - y_1 = m(x - x_1). \] This is the point-slope form. It is the easiest form to use when you know a point and the slope.

Example 2

From a point and a slope

Write the equation of the line in slope-intercept form.

  1. Slope \(4\), passes through \((2, -1)\).
  2. Slope \(-\tfrac{2}{3}\), passes through \((6, 5)\).

(a) Point-slope: \(y - (-1) = 4(x - 2)\), i.e. \(y + 1 = 4x - 8\). Solve: \(y = 4x - 9\).

(b) \(y - 5 = -\tfrac{2}{3}(x - 6)\) gives \(y = -\tfrac{2}{3}x + 4 + 5 = -\tfrac{2}{3}x + 9\).

Example 3

From two points

Find an equation for the line through \((-2, 7)\) and \((4, -5)\). Give your answer in slope-intercept form.

Compute the slope: \(m = \dfrac{-5 - 7}{4 - (-2)} = \dfrac{-12}{6} = -2\).

Use point-slope with \((4, -5)\): \(y + 5 = -2(x - 4)\). Distribute and solve: \(y = -2x + 8 - 5 = -2x + 3\).

Standard Form, Horizontal, and Vertical Lines

Standard Form

Any line can be written as \(Ax + By = C\) for constants \(A\), \(B\), \(C\) (with \(A\) and \(B\) not both zero). This is called the standard form of a linear equation.

Horizontal and Vertical Lines
  • The horizontal line through \((a, b)\) is \(y = b\) (slope 0).
  • The vertical line through \((a, b)\) is \(x = a\) (slope undefined). It is not the graph of a function.
Example 4

Horizontal and vertical

Write equations for:

  1. The horizontal line through \((-3, 5)\).
  2. The vertical line through \((-3, 5)\).
  3. The line with \(y\)-intercept \(2\) that is parallel to the \(x\)-axis.

(a) \(y = 5\).

(b) \(x = -3\).

(c) Parallel to the \(x\)-axis means horizontal, so \(y = 2\).

Parallel and Perpendicular Lines

Theorem — Parallel and Perpendicular Slopes

Let \(L_1\) have slope \(m_1\) and \(L_2\) have slope \(m_2\) (both non-vertical).

  • \(L_1\) and \(L_2\) are parallel iff \(m_1 = m_2\).
  • \(L_1\) and \(L_2\) are perpendicular iff \(m_1 m_2 = -1\), i.e. \(m_2 = -\dfrac{1}{m_1}\).

Vertical and horizontal lines are perpendicular.

Example 5

Parallel and perpendicular

Find the equation of each line.

  1. Through \((1, 4)\), parallel to \(y = -3x + 2\).
  2. Through \((1, 4)\), perpendicular to \(y = -3x + 2\).
  3. Through \((-2, -1)\), parallel to the line \(2x + 5y = 10\).

(a) Same slope \(-3\). Point-slope: \(y - 4 = -3(x - 1)\), so \(y = -3x + 7\).

(b) Perpendicular slope is \(\tfrac{1}{3}\) (negative reciprocal). Point-slope: \(y - 4 = \tfrac{1}{3}(x - 1)\), so \(y = \tfrac{1}{3}x + \tfrac{11}{3}\).

(c) Solve for slope: \(5y = -2x + 10\), \(y = -\tfrac{2}{5}x + 2\). Slope is \(-\tfrac{2}{5}\). Point-slope: \(y - (-1) = -\tfrac{2}{5}(x - (-2))\) gives \(y = -\tfrac{2}{5}x - \tfrac{4}{5} - 1 = -\tfrac{2}{5}x - \tfrac{9}{5}\).
