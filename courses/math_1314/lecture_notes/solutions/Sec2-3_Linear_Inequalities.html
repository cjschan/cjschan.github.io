Section 2.3

Linear Inequalities

Worked Solutions · View student notes →

A linear inequality is solved much like a linear equation, with one important new rule: when you multiply or divide by a negative number, the inequality direction flips. We’ll also handle compound inequalities and express solutions in interval notation.

Solving Linear Inequalities

Properties of Inequality

Let \(a\), \(b\), \(c\) be real numbers.

  • Add or subtract the same number on both sides — direction unchanged.
  • Multiply or divide by a positive number — direction unchanged.
  • Multiply or divide by a negative number — direction flips.
Example 1

Basic linear inequalities

Solve and write the solution in interval notation.

  1. \(3x - 5 \leq 7\)
  2. \(2 - 4x > 14\)
  3. \(\dfrac{x}{2} - 1 \geq \dfrac{x}{3} + 2\)

(a) Add 5: \(3x \leq 12\). Divide by 3: \(x \leq 4\). Solution: \((-\infty, 4]\).

(b) Subtract 2: \(-4x > 12\). Divide by \(-4\) (flip!): \(x < -3\). Solution: \((-\infty, -3)\).

(c) Multiply by 6: \(3x - 6 \geq 2x + 12\). Subtract \(2x\): \(x - 6 \geq 12\), so \(x \geq 18\). Solution: \([18, \infty)\).

Compound Inequalities

Compound Inequality

A compound inequality joins two inequalities with the word and (intersection) or or (union). The shorthand \(a < x < b\) means \(a < x\) and \(x < b\).

Example 2

Three-part inequality

Solve \(-3 < 2x + 1 \leq 7\). Write the solution in interval notation.

Subtract 1 from each part: \(-4 < 2x \leq 6\). Divide by 2: \(-2 < x \leq 3\). Solution: \((-2, 3]\).

Example 3

“And” vs. “or”

Solve and graph each.

  1. \(2x + 3 < 11\) and \(x - 1 \geq -4\)
  2. \(x + 2 \leq -1\) or \(2x - 3 > 5\)

(a) Solve each: \(x < 4\) and \(x \geq -3\). Intersect: \([-3, 4)\).

(b) Solve each: \(x \leq -3\) or \(x > 4\). Union: \((-\infty, -3] \cup (4, \infty)\).

Linear Inequalities in Applications

Example 4

Test scores

Linda’s exam scores are 78, 85, and 81. The final exam counts as one test grade. To finish with an average of at least 82, what is the minimum score she needs on the final?

Let \(f\) be the final exam score. We need \(\dfrac{78 + 85 + 81 + f}{4} \geq 82\). Multiply by 4: \(244 + f \geq 328\), so \(f \geq 84\). She needs at least an 84.

Example 5

Phone plan comparison

Plan A costs $30 per month plus $0.10 per text. Plan B costs $20 per month plus $0.20 per text. For how many texts \(t\) per month is Plan A cheaper than Plan B?

We need \(30 + 0.10 t < 20 + 0.20 t\). Subtract \(0.10 t\) and \(20\): \(10 < 0.10 t\), so \(t > 100\). Plan A is cheaper when you send more than 100 texts per month.
