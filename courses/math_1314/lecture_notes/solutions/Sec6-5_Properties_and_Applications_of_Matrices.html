Section 6.5

Properties and Applications of Matrices

Worked Solutions

Matrices aren’t just for solving systems. Today we develop the algebra of matrices: addition, scalar multiplication, and multiplication, and use matrices to encode and manipulate data and equations.

Matrix Notation

Matrix

A matrix is a rectangular array of numbers organized into rows and columns. A matrix with \(m\) rows and \(n\) columns has dimension \(m \times n\). The entry in row \(i\), column \(j\) is denoted \(a_{ij}\).

A matrix with the same number of rows as columns is square.

Addition and Scalar Multiplication

Rules
  • Two matrices can be added only if they have the same dimension. Add component-by-component.
  • To multiply by a scalar, multiply every entry by the scalar.
  • The zero matrix \(\mathbf{0}\) is the additive identity: \(A + \mathbf{0} = A\).
Example 1

Add and scale

Let \(A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & -1 \\ 0 & 4 \end{bmatrix}\) and \(B = \begin{bmatrix} 5 & 3 \\ -2 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\). Compute.

  1. \(A + B\)
  2. \(3A\)
  3. \(2A - B\)

(a) \(A + B = \begin{bmatrix} 7 & 2 \\ -2 & 5 \end{bmatrix}\).

(b) \(3A = \begin{bmatrix} 6 & -3 \\ 0 & 12 \end{bmatrix}\).

(c) \(2A - B = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & -2 \\ 0 & 8 \end{bmatrix} - \begin{bmatrix} 5 & 3 \\ -2 & 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -1 & -5 \\ 2 & 7 \end{bmatrix}\).

Matrix Multiplication

Matrix Product

If \(A\) is \(m \times n\) and \(B\) is \(n \times p\), the product \(AB\) is \(m \times p\). The entry in row \(i\), column \(j\) of \(AB\) is the dot product of row \(i\) of \(A\) with column \(j\) of \(B\):

\((AB)_{ij} = \sum_{k=1}^{n} a_{ik} b_{kj}\).

Caution: Matrix multiplication is generally not commutative: usually \(AB \neq BA\).

Example 2

Multiply two matrices

Compute \(AB\) where \(A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 3 & -1 \end{bmatrix}\) and \(B = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 0 \\ -2 & 5 \end{bmatrix}\).

Each entry of \(AB\) is row-of-\(A\) dotted with column-of-\(B\):

  • (1, 1): \(2(4) + 1(-2) = 6\)
  • (1, 2): \(2(0) + 1(5) = 5\)
  • (2, 1): \(3(4) + (-1)(-2) = 14\)
  • (2, 2): \(3(0) + (-1)(5) = -5\)

\(AB = \begin{bmatrix} 6 & 5 \\ 14 & -5 \end{bmatrix}\).

Example 3

Dimensions matter

Let \(C\) be a \(2 \times 3\) matrix and \(D\) be a \(3 \times 4\) matrix. Which products are defined?

  1. \(CD\)
  2. \(DC\)
  3. \(CC\)

(a) \(CD\) is defined: inner dimensions match (3 and 3). Result is \(2 \times 4\).

(b) \(DC\) is undefined: inner dimensions \(4\) and \(2\) don’t match.

(c) \(CC\) is undefined: \(2 \times 3\) and \(2 \times 3\) don’t match.

The Identity and Inverse Matrices

Identity Matrix

The \(n \times n\) identity matrix \(I_n\) has 1’s on the main diagonal and 0’s elsewhere. For any \(n \times n\) matrix \(A\), \(AI_n = I_n A = A\).

Inverse

An \(n \times n\) matrix \(A\) is invertible if there exists \(A^{-1}\) with \(A A^{-1} = A^{-1} A = I_n\). For a \(2 \times 2\) matrix \(A = \begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{bmatrix}\) with \(\det A = ad - bc \neq 0\), \[ A^{-1} = \dfrac{1}{ad - bc}\begin{bmatrix} d & -b \\ -c & a \end{bmatrix}. \]

Example 4

Inverse of a 2×2 matrix

Find \(A^{-1}\) for \(A = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 7 \\ 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix}\).

\(\det A = (4)(2) - (7)(1) = 1\). \(A^{-1} = \dfrac{1}{1}\begin{bmatrix} 2 & -7 \\ -1 & 4 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & -7 \\ -1 & 4 \end{bmatrix}\).

Check: \(A A^{-1} = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 7 \\ 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} 2 & -7 \\ -1 & 4 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\) ✓.

Solving Systems with Inverses

Matrix Equation

A linear system \(A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}\) (with \(A\) the coefficient matrix and \(\mathbf{x}\), \(\mathbf{b}\) column vectors) has solution \(\mathbf{x} = A^{-1}\mathbf{b}\), provided \(A\) is invertible.

Example 5

Using the inverse

Use the inverse from Example 4 to solve \(\begin{cases} 4x + 7y = 11 \\ x + 2y = 3 \end{cases}\).

\(\mathbf{x} = A^{-1}\mathbf{b} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & -7 \\ -1 & 4 \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} 11 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 22 - 21 \\ -11 + 12 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\).

Solution: \((x, y) = (1, 1)\).

An Application: Encoded Data

Example 6

Inventory by store

A coffee chain has three stores. The matrix \(N\) gives the number of bags of each of two roasts (R1, R2) sold this month at each store, and the column vector \(\mathbf{p}\) gives the price of each roast. Compute the monthly revenue per store.

\(N = \begin{bmatrix} 80 & 60 \\ 50 & 90 \\ 100 & 70 \end{bmatrix}, \quad \mathbf{p} = \begin{bmatrix} 12 \\ 15 \end{bmatrix}\)

Each row of \(N\) is one store, each column is one roast. Revenue per store is \(N\mathbf{p}\).

Store 1: \(80(12) + 60(15) = 960 + 900 = 1860\).

Store 2: \(50(12) + 90(15) = 600 + 1350 = 1950\).

Store 3: \(100(12) + 70(15) = 1200 + 1050 = 2250\).

\(N\mathbf{p} = \begin{bmatrix} 1860 \\ 1950 \\ 2250 \end{bmatrix}\) dollars.
