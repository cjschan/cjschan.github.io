Section 6.4

Solutions to Linear Systems Using Matrices

Worked Solutions

A matrix is a rectangular array of numbers. We can encode any linear system as an augmented matrix and use Gaussian elimination — a sequence of row operations — to solve it systematically. This scales much better than ad-hoc elimination.

The Augmented Matrix

Augmented Matrix

The augmented matrix of a linear system places the coefficients of the variables to the left of a vertical bar and the constants to the right. For example, the system \(\begin{cases} x + 2y = 5 \\ 3x - y = 4 \end{cases}\) has augmented matrix \(\left[\begin{array}{cc|c} 1 & 2 & 5 \\ 3 & -1 & 4 \end{array}\right].\)

Row Operations

Three Elementary Row Operations
  1. Swap two rows.
  2. Multiply a row by a non-zero constant.
  3. Add a multiple of one row to another row.

Each operation produces an equivalent system — same solutions.

Row-Echelon Form

Row-Echelon Form

A matrix is in row-echelon form when:

  • Any rows of all zeros are at the bottom.
  • The first non-zero entry in each non-zero row (its leading entry) is to the right of the leading entry of the row above.
  • Often the leading entry is 1.

Reduced row-echelon form (RREF) additionally requires each leading 1 to be the only non-zero entry in its column.

Gaussian Elimination

Example 1

Solve a 2 × 2 system

Solve using row operations.

\(\begin{cases} x + 2y = 5 \\ 3x - y = 4 \end{cases}\)

Augmented: \(\left[\begin{array}{cc|c} 1 & 2 & 5 \\ 3 & -1 & 4 \end{array}\right]\).

Row 2 → Row 2 \(- 3\)·Row 1: \(\left[\begin{array}{cc|c} 1 & 2 & 5 \\ 0 & -7 & -11 \end{array}\right]\).

Row 2 → \(-\tfrac{1}{7}\)·Row 2: \(\left[\begin{array}{cc|c} 1 & 2 & 5 \\ 0 & 1 & \tfrac{11}{7} \end{array}\right]\). Back-substitute: \(y = \tfrac{11}{7}\), and \(x + 2 \cdot \tfrac{11}{7} = 5\), so \(x = \tfrac{13}{7}\).

Solution: \(\left(\tfrac{13}{7}, \tfrac{11}{7}\right)\).

Example 2

Solve a 3 × 3 system

Solve.

\(\begin{cases} x - y + z = 2 \\ 2x + y - z = 1 \\ -x + 2y + z = 0 \end{cases}\)

Augmented: \(\left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & -1 & 1 & 2 \\ 2 & 1 & -1 & 1 \\ -1 & 2 & 1 & 0 \end{array}\right]\).

R2 → R2 − 2R1, R3 → R3 + R1:

\(\left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & -1 & 1 & 2 \\ 0 & 3 & -3 & -3 \\ 0 & 1 & 2 & 2 \end{array}\right]\).

R2 → \(\tfrac{1}{3}\)R2, then R3 → R3 − R2:

\(\left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & -1 & 1 & 2 \\ 0 & 1 & -1 & -1 \\ 0 & 0 & 3 & 3 \end{array}\right]\).

Back-substitute: \(z = 1\); then \(y - z = -1\) gives \(y = 0\); then \(x - 0 + 1 = 2\), so \(x = 1\).

Solution: \((1, 0, 1)\).

Example 3

Inconsistent system

Solve.

\(\begin{cases} x + y + z = 1 \\ 2x + 2y + 2z = 5 \\ x - y + z = 0 \end{cases}\)

Augmented: \(\left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 \\ 2 & 2 & 2 & 5 \\ 1 & -1 & 1 & 0 \end{array}\right]\). R2 → R2 − 2R1: \(0 = 3\). The row \([0\;0\;0\,|\,3]\) means “\(0 = 3\)”, a contradiction. No solution.

Recognizing Solutions in RREF

Reading the Outcome
  • Every variable has a leading 1 in its column ⇒ unique solution.
  • A variable has no leading 1 ⇒ infinitely many solutions; that variable becomes a parameter.
  • A row of the form \([0\;0\;\cdots\;0\,|\,c]\) with \(c \neq 0\) ⇒ no solution.
Example 4

Parameterized solution

Solve.

\(\begin{cases} x + y - z = 4 \\ 2x + 2y - 2z = 8 \\ -x - y + z = -4 \end{cases}\)

All three equations are scalar multiples of the first. Augmented matrix reduces to one non-zero row: \(\left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 1 & -1 & 4 \end{array}\right]\), i.e. \(x + y - z = 4\).

Two free parameters. Let \(y = s, z = t\). Then \(x = 4 - s + t\).

Solution: \((4 - s + t,\; s,\; t)\) for any real \(s, t\).
