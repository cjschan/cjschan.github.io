Section 5.3

Exponential Functions and Models

Worked Solutions

Exponential functions describe processes where the rate of change is proportional to the current amount — populations, money compounding, radioactive decay, and many natural phenomena. Today we identify, evaluate, and graph exponential functions, and use them to model real-world growth and decay.

Exponential Functions

Exponential Function

An exponential function with base \(a\) has the form \[ f(x) = a^x, \] where \(a > 0\) and \(a \neq 1\). The variable is in the exponent.

Properties of \(f(x) = a^x\)
  • Domain: all real numbers; range: \((0, \infty)\).
  • \(y\)-intercept: \((0, 1)\), since \(a^0 = 1\).
  • The horizontal asymptote is \(y = 0\) (the \(x\)-axis).
  • If \(a > 1\): the graph is increasing (exponential growth).
  • If \(0 < a < 1\): the graph is decreasing (exponential decay).
Example 1

Evaluating

Let \(f(x) = 3^x\) and \(g(x) = \left(\dfrac{1}{2}\right)^x\).

  1. Find \(f(2)\), \(f(0)\), \(f(-1)\).
  2. Find \(g(3)\), \(g(0)\), \(g(-2)\).

(a) \(f(2) = 9\); \(f(0) = 1\); \(f(-1) = \tfrac{1}{3}\).

(b) \(g(3) = \tfrac{1}{8}\); \(g(0) = 1\); \(g(-2) = 2^2 = 4\).

Compound Interest

Compound Interest Formula

If a principal \(P\) is invested at annual interest rate \(r\) (as a decimal), compounded \(n\) times per year, the value after \(t\) years is \[ A = P\left(1 + \dfrac{r}{n}\right)^{nt}. \] When compounded continuously: \(A = P e^{rt}\), where \(e \approx 2.71828\) is the natural base.

Example 2

Compounding monthly vs. continuously

$5{,}000 is invested at 6% annual interest for 10 years.

  1. Find the value if interest is compounded monthly.
  2. Find the value if interest is compounded continuously.

(a) \(A = 5000\left(1 + \dfrac{0.06}{12}\right)^{120} = 5000(1.005)^{120} \approx 5000(1.81940) \approx \$9{,}096.98\).

(b) \(A = 5000 e^{0.06 \cdot 10} = 5000 e^{0.6} \approx 5000(1.82212) \approx \$9{,}110.59\).

Exponential Models

General Exponential Model

An exponential growth or decay model has the form \[ A(t) = A_0 \cdot b^t, \] where \(A_0\) is the initial amount and \(b\) is the per-unit-time growth factor. \(b > 1\) means growth; \(0 < b < 1\) means decay.

Example 3

Population growth

A bacteria culture starts with 100 cells and doubles every 3 hours.

  1. Write a model \(P(t)\) for the population after \(t\) hours.
  2. How many cells are present after 12 hours?
  3. How many after 24 hours?

(a) Doubling every 3 hours means \(P(t) = 100 \cdot 2^{t/3}\).

(b) \(P(12) = 100 \cdot 2^{4} = 100 \cdot 16 = 1{,}600\) cells.

(c) \(P(24) = 100 \cdot 2^{8} = 25{,}600\) cells.

Example 4

Radioactive decay

The radioactive isotope iodine-131 has a half-life of about 8 days. If a sample starts at 40 g, how much remains after 24 days?

Half-life model: \(A(t) = 40 \cdot \left(\dfrac{1}{2}\right)^{t/8}\). Then \(A(24) = 40 \cdot (1/2)^3 = 40 \cdot \tfrac{1}{8} = 5\) g.

The Natural Base \(e\)

Natural Exponential

The number \(e \approx 2.71828\) is irrational and arises naturally from continuous compounding and many calculus formulas. The function \(f(x) = e^x\) is called the natural exponential function.

Example 5

Continuous decay

The mass of a substance decays according to \(m(t) = 200 e^{-0.05 t}\), where \(t\) is in years. Find \(m(0)\), \(m(20)\), and the limit as \(t \to \infty\).

\(m(0) = 200 e^0 = 200\) g.

\(m(20) = 200 e^{-1} \approx 200(0.3679) \approx 73.58\) g.

As \(t \to \infty\), \(e^{-0.05 t} \to 0\), so \(m(t) \to 0\).
