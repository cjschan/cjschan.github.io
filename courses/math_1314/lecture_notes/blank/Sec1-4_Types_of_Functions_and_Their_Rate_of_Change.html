Section 1.4

Types of Functions and Their Rate of Change

Student Notes

We classify functions by how their outputs change as the input changes. Today we look at constant and linear functions, define slope as a rate of change, and extend the idea to the average rate of change for any function.

Constant and Linear Functions

Constant Function

A function of the form \(f(x) = b\) is a constant function. Its graph is a horizontal line through the point \((0, b)\). Every input is mapped to the same output \(b\).

Linear Function

A function of the form \(f(x) = mx + b\) is a linear function. Its graph is a straight line with slope \(m\) and \(y\)-intercept \((0, b)\).

Example 1

Constant vs. linear

Classify each function as constant, linear, or neither.

  1. \(f(x) = -7\)
  2. \(g(x) = 4x - 3\)
  3. \(h(x) = x^2 + 1\)
  4. \(p(x) = 2 - \tfrac{1}{3} x\)

Slope as a Rate of Change

Slope

The slope of the line through two points \((x_1, y_1)\) and \((x_2, y_2)\) is \[ m = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1} = \frac{\text{change in } y}{\text{change in } x}. \] Slope measures the constant rate of change of \(y\) with respect to \(x\).

Interpreting Slope
  • If \(m > 0\), the line rises from left to right (the function is increasing).
  • If \(m < 0\), the line falls from left to right (decreasing).
  • If \(m = 0\), the line is horizontal (constant).
  • If the slope is undefined, the line is vertical (and is not a function).
Example 2

Computing slope

Find the slope of the line through each pair of points.

  1. \((1, 2)\) and \((4, 11)\)
  2. \((-2, 5)\) and \((3, -5)\)
  3. \((6, 4)\) and \((-2, 4)\)
  4. \((3, 1)\) and \((3, -7)\)
Example 3

Slope as a real-world rate

A car’s gas tank holds 16 gallons. After driving 90 miles the tank has 13 gallons. Assume gas is consumed at a constant rate.

  1. What is the rate of change of gas remaining with respect to miles driven?
  2. Interpret the rate in plain English.

Average Rate of Change

Average Rate of Change

For a function \(f\), the average rate of change from \(x = a\) to \(x = b\) (with \(a \neq b\)) is \[ \frac{f(b) - f(a)}{b - a}. \] Geometrically, this is the slope of the line through \((a, f(a))\) and \((b, f(b))\) on the graph of \(f\) (the secant line).

Example 4

Average rate of change of a parabola

Let \(f(x) = x^2\).

  1. Find the average rate of change from \(x = 1\) to \(x = 3\).
  2. Find the average rate of change from \(x = -2\) to \(x = 0\).
  3. Why are the two answers different?

Increasing, Decreasing, and Constant

Behavior on an Interval

On an interval \(I\), a function \(f\) is

  • increasing if \(f(x_1) < f(x_2)\) whenever \(x_1 < x_2\) in \(I\),
  • decreasing if \(f(x_1) > f(x_2)\) whenever \(x_1 < x_2\) in \(I\),
  • constant if \(f(x_1) = f(x_2)\) for all \(x_1, x_2\) in \(I\).

For a smooth function, increasing intervals correspond to positive average rates of change, decreasing to negative.

Reading behavior from a graph
