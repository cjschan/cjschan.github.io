Section 3.2

Quadratic Equations and Problem Solving

Student Notes

A quadratic equation has the form \(ax^2 + bx + c = 0\). Today we solve quadratic equations four ways — factoring, the square-root property, completing the square, and the quadratic formula — and use them in word problems.

Solving by Factoring

Zero-Product Property

If \(AB = 0\), then \(A = 0\) or \(B = 0\) (or both). To solve a factored quadratic, set each factor equal to zero and solve.

Example 1

Factor and solve

Solve.

  1. \(x^2 - x - 12 = 0\)
  2. \(2x^2 + 7x = 4\)
  3. \(9x^2 = 25\)

The Square-Root Property

Square-Root Property

If \(X^2 = c\) with \(c \geq 0\), then \(X = \sqrt{c}\) or \(X = -\sqrt{c}\), often written \(X = \pm\sqrt{c}\).

Example 2

Square-root method

Solve.

  1. \((x - 4)^2 = 49\)
  2. \(2(x + 1)^2 - 18 = 0\)

Completing the Square

Completing the Square

To solve \(x^2 + bx + c = 0\) by completing the square: move \(c\) to the other side, then add \(\left(\dfrac{b}{2}\right)^2\) to both sides so the left side becomes a perfect square \(\left(x + \dfrac{b}{2}\right)^2\). Then apply the square-root property.

Example 3

Complete the square

Solve \(x^2 + 6x - 5 = 0\) by completing the square.

The Quadratic Formula

Quadratic Formula

The solutions of \(ax^2 + bx + c = 0\) (with \(a \neq 0\)) are \[ x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}. \] The expression \(D = b^2 - 4ac\) under the radical is the discriminant.

  • \(D > 0\): two distinct real solutions.
  • \(D = 0\): one repeated real solution.
  • \(D < 0\): no real solutions (two complex solutions).
Example 4

Using the quadratic formula

Solve.

  1. \(2x^2 - 5x - 3 = 0\)
  2. \(x^2 + 4x + 5 = 0\)
  3. \(3x^2 - 12x + 12 = 0\)
