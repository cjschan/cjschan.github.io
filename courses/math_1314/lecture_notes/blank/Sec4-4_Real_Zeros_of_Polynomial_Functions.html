Section 4.4

Real Zeros of Polynomial Functions

Student Notes

How do we actually find the zeros of a higher-degree polynomial? We’ll use the rational zeros theorem to generate a finite list of candidates, then synthetic or long division to confirm zeros and reduce the polynomial to a quadratic we can solve.

Polynomial Division

Division Algorithm

If \(f(x)\) is divided by a non-zero polynomial \(d(x)\), there exist a unique quotient \(q(x)\) and remainder \(r(x)\) with \(f(x) = d(x) q(x) + r(x)\), where \(\deg r < \deg d\). Long division and synthetic division both implement this.

Example 1

Synthetic division

Use synthetic division to compute \((2x^3 - 5x^2 + 3x - 4) \div (x - 2)\).

Remainder and Factor Theorems

Remainder Theorem

When \(f(x)\) is divided by \((x - c)\), the remainder equals \(f(c)\).

Factor Theorem

\((x - c)\) is a factor of \(f(x)\) if and only if \(f(c) = 0\).

Example 2

Verifying a zero

Show that \(x = 3\) is a zero of \(f(x) = x^3 - 4x^2 + x + 6\) and use it to factor \(f\) completely.

The Rational Zeros Theorem

Rational Zeros Theorem

If a polynomial \(f\) with integer coefficients has a rational zero \(\dfrac{p}{q}\) (in lowest terms), then \(p\) divides the constant term \(a_0\) and \(q\) divides the leading coefficient \(a_n\).

Example 3

Listing rational candidates

List the possible rational zeros of \(f(x) = 2x^3 - x^2 - 8x + 4\).
