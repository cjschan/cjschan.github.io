Section 1.3

Experiments

Association does not imply causation. Randomized experiments are the gold standard for establishing cause and effect.

Association vs. Causation

Two variables can be related without one causing the other.

Associated

  • Values of one variable tend to be related to values of the other.
  • Example: countries with more TVs per capita have higher life expectancy.

Causally Associated

  • Changing the explanatory variable actually influences the response.
  • Buying more TVs does not make you live longer.
Association is not necessarily causal! A hidden third variable (wealth) explains both TVs and life expectancy.

Confounding Variables

A third variable associated with both the explanatory and response variables.

What Is a Confounder?

  • It is related to the explanatory variable.
  • It is also related to the response variable.
  • It offers a plausible alternative explanation for the association.

Example

TVs per capita vs. life expectancy:
  • Wealth is a confounding variable.
  • Wealthier countries buy more TVs and have better health care.
  • We cannot conclude TVs cause longer life.

Observational Study vs. Experiment

The key difference is whether the researcher controls the explanatory variable.

Observational Study

  • The researcher does not control any variable.
  • Simply observes values as they naturally exist.
  • Confounding variables are almost always present.
  • Cannot establish causation.

Experiment

  • The researcher actively controls the explanatory variable.
  • Assigns treatments to subjects.
  • If randomized, confounding variables are eliminated.
  • Can establish causation.

Randomized Experiments

Randomly assigning treatments eliminates confounding variables.

How It Works

  • Randomly divide subjects into groups.
  • Assign a different treatment to each group.
  • Because assignment is random, treatment groups should look similar on all other characteristics.

Why It Works

  • The explanatory variable is not associated with any confounding variable.
  • Any difference in the response can be attributed to the treatment.
  • Randomized experiments are very powerful: they allow you to infer causality.

Control Groups and the Placebo Effect

A comparison group is essential. People often improve just because they think they are being treated.

Control Group

  • A group that does not receive the active treatment.
  • Provides a baseline for comparison.
  • Without it, we cannot tell if the treatment actually worked.

Placebo Effect

  • People experience the effect they expect, even without real treatment.
  • In one study, fake knee surgery relieved pain just as much as real surgery.
  • A placebo (fake treatment) and blinding help control for this.

Randomization in Data Collection

Random sampling and random assignment serve different purposes.

Random Sample?

  • Yes: possible to generalize to the population.
  • No: should not generalize to the population.

Random Assignment?

  • Yes: possible to make conclusions about causality.
  • No: cannot make conclusions about causality.
Ideal but rare: a randomized experiment on a random sample. If the focus is causation, you need random assignment. If the focus is estimating a population value, you need a random sample.

What to Remember

The type of study determines what conclusions are possible.

Core Ideas

  • Association does not imply causation.
  • Confounding variables provide alternative explanations.
  • Observational studies cannot establish causation.

Experiments

  • Randomized experiments prevent confounding.
  • A control or comparison group is necessary.
  • The placebo effect exists, so blinding should be used.
