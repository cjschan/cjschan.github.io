Inference for a Mean

Confidence intervals and hypothesis tests for one population mean.

\[ \text{sample statistic} \pm t^* \cdot SE \]

From z to t

For means, the main new issue is that the population standard deviation is usually unknown.

If the Statistic Is Normal

  • A confidence interval still has the form statistic \(\pm\) critical value \(\cdot SE\).
  • A test statistic still compares the sample to the null in standard error units.
  • If we knew \(\sigma\), we could use the standard normal distribution.
\(t\)
Because \(\sigma\) is unknown, we replace it with \(s\). That changes the reference distribution from \(z\) to \(t\).

Standard Error for a Mean

The standard error measures how much sample means vary from sample to sample.

Population Formula

\[ SE(\bar{x})=\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} \]
This is the true standard error, but it requires the population standard deviation \(\sigma\).

What We Actually Use

\[ SE(\bar{x})=\frac{s}{\sqrt{n}} \]
Replacing \(\sigma\) with \(s\) adds uncertainty, so we use a t-distribution instead of a normal distribution.

When Can We Use t?

The t procedures work when the sample is large, or when the data are approximately normal.

\(n \ge 30\)
For large samples, the CLT makes the sampling distribution of \(\bar{x}\) approximately normal.
\(df=n-1\)
The t-distribution depends on degrees of freedom. Higher \(df\) makes \(t\) closer to normal.
Small \(n\)
If the sample is small, only use t when the data look reasonably symmetric with no extreme outliers.
t vs z
As the degrees of freedom increase, the \(t\)-distribution gets closer to the standard normal distribution.

Confidence Interval for a Mean

For a one-sample mean, use the sample mean, a t critical value, and the standard error based on \(s\).

\[ \bar{x} \pm t^* \cdot \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}} \]
  • \(t^*\) comes from a t-distribution with \(df=n-1\).
  • Use this when the sample is large or the data are approximately normal.
  • The margin of error is \(t^* \cdot \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}\).

Gribbles Example Setup

  • Sample size: \(n=50\)
  • Sample mean: \(\bar{x}=3.1\) mm
  • Sample standard deviation: \(s=0.72\) mm
  • Goal: a \(90\%\) confidence interval for the average gribble length

Confidence Interval Example

A sample of \(50\) gribbles has average length \(3.1\) mm with standard deviation \(0.72\) mm.

Step 1
Find the degrees of freedom and the t critical value for a \(90\%\) confidence interval.
\[ df=50-1=49,\qquad t^* \approx 1.677 \]
Step 2
Compute the standard error.
\[ SE=\frac{0.72}{\sqrt{50}} \approx 0.102 \]
Step 3
Compute the margin of error.
\[ ME=1.677(0.102)\approx 0.171 \]
Step 4
Build the interval around the sample mean.
\[ 3.1 \pm 0.171=(2.929,\ 3.271) \]
Interpretation: We are \(90\%\) confident that the average length of gribbles is between \(2.929\) mm and \(3.271\) mm.

Hypothesis Testing for a Mean

For a mean, the test statistic uses a t-distribution with \(df=n-1\).

Test Statistic

\[ t=\frac{\bar{x}-\mu_0}{s/\sqrt{n}} \]
The numerator compares the sample mean to the null mean \(\mu_0\).

p-Value

  • The p-value is the area in the tail(s) beyond the observed \(t\).
  • Use a t-distribution with \(df=n-1\).
  • The tail direction comes from the alternative hypothesis.

Hypothesis Test Example

Chips Ahoy! advertised at least \(1000\) chocolate chips per bag. A sample of \(42\) bags was counted.

Step 1
Set up the hypotheses for checking whether the average number of chips is greater than \(1000\).
\[ H_0:\mu=1000 \qquad H_a:\mu>1000 \]
Step 2
Check conditions and record the sample statistics: \(n=42\), \(\bar{x}=1261.6\), and \(s=117.6\).
With \(n=42\), the sample size is large, so the t test is appropriate.
Step 3
Compute the t statistic.
\[ t=\frac{1261.6-1000}{117.6/\sqrt{42}} \approx 14.42 \]
Steps 4-5
Find the right-tail p-value and interpret in context.
Conclusion: We reject the null hypothesis \(H_0\). We have statistically significant evidence that the average number of chocolate chips per bag is greater than \(1000\).

What to Remember

For means, the big difference is replacing \(\sigma\) with \(s\), which leads to the t-distribution.

Confidence Interval

\[ \bar{x} \pm t^* \cdot \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}} \]
  • Use \(df=n-1\).
  • Need a large sample or approximately normal data.
  • Interpret the interval as a plausible range for the population mean.

Hypothesis Test

\[ t=\frac{\bar{x}-\mu_0}{s/\sqrt{n}} \]
  • Use a t-distribution with \(df=n-1\).
  • The p-value is the tail area beyond the observed \(t\).
  • Small p-values give evidence against \(H_0\).
