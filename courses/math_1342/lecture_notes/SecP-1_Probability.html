Section P.1

Probability Rules

Events, probabilities, addition rules, conditional probability, and independence.

Events and Probability

An event is any collection of outcomes. Its probability is the long-run proportion of times it occurs.

Probability Basics

  • \(P(A)\) is always between 0 and 1.
  • \(P(A) = 1\) means A will definitely happen.
  • \(P(A) = 0\) means A will definitely not happen.

Equally Likely Outcomes

\[ P(A) = \frac{\text{number of outcomes in } A}{\text{total number of outcomes}} \]
Example: \(P(\text{rolling a 5}) = 1/6\)

The Addition Rule

For the probability of A or B, we must avoid double-counting the overlap.

General Addition Rule

\[ P(A \text{ or } B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \text{ and } B) \]
Subtract the overlap to avoid counting it twice.

Disjoint Events

If A and B cannot both happen (mutually exclusive):
\[ P(A \text{ or } B) = P(A) + P(B) \]
Example: drawing a heart or a spade from one card.

Example: Rock and Roll Hall of Fame

273 inductees classified by performer status and whether the group has female members.

Applying the Addition Rule

\(P(\text{female or performer})\):
\[ \frac{41}{273} + \frac{181}{273} - \frac{32}{273} = \frac{190}{273} \approx 0.696 \]

The Complement Rule

The probability of "not A" is one minus the probability of A.

Formula

\[ P(\text{not } A) = 1 - P(A) \]
Events A and "not A" are disjoint and together cover all possibilities.

Example

52% drink caffeine in the morning, 47% in the afternoon, 37% both.
\[ P(\text{morning or afternoon}) = 0.52 + 0.47 - 0.37 = 0.62 \]
\[ P(\text{neither}) = 1 - 0.62 = 0.38 \]

Conditional Probability

The probability of A given that B has occurred.

Formula

\[ P(A \mid B) = \frac{P(A \text{ and } B)}{P(B)} \]
Read as "probability of A given B."

Example

\(P(\text{female} \mid \text{performer})\):
\[ \frac{32/273}{181/273} = \frac{32}{181} \approx 0.177 \]
Caution: \(P(\text{female} \mid \text{performer}) = 0.177\) is not the same as \(P(\text{performer} \mid \text{female}) = 32/41 = 0.780\).

The Multiplication Rule and Independence

Finding the probability that two events both happen.

General Multiplication Rule

\[ P(A \text{ and } B) = P(A) \cdot P(B \mid A) \]

If A and B Are Independent

Knowing A happened does not change the probability of B.
\[ P(A \text{ and } B) = P(A) \cdot P(B) \]
Example: flipping heads and drawing a heart are independent.

Independence Examples

Replacement matters when drawing from a finite set.

With Replacement (Independent)

\(P(\text{two aces from a deck, with replacement})\):
\[ \frac{4}{52} \times \frac{4}{52} = \frac{16}{2704} \approx 0.0059 \]

Without Replacement (Not Independent)

\(P(\text{two aces, without replacement})\):
\[ \frac{4}{52} \times \frac{3}{51} = \frac{12}{2652} \approx 0.0045 \]

What to Remember

The core probability rules.

Key Formulas

  • Addition: \(P(A \text{ or } B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \text{ and } B)\)
  • Complement: \(P(\text{not } A) = 1 - P(A)\)
  • Conditional: \(P(A \mid B) = P(A \text{ and } B) / P(B)\)
  • Multiplication: \(P(A \text{ and } B) = P(A) \cdot P(B \mid A)\)

Key Concepts

  • Disjoint: cannot both happen; \(P(A \text{ and } B) = 0\)
  • Independent: knowing one does not change the other; \(P(B \mid A) = P(B)\)
  • Disjoint events are not independent (unless one has probability 0).
