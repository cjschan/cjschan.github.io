Section 3.2

Understanding and Interpreting
Confidence Intervals

Moving from a single point estimate to a range of plausible values

Motivation

From Point Estimates to Interval Estimates

A point estimate is almost never exactly right. Can we do better?

Point Estimate Limitation

  • A single number (\(\bar{x}\) or \(\hat{p}\)) as our best guess
  • Almost certainly not exactly equal to the parameter
  • Gives no indication of how close it might be

Interval Estimate

  • A range of plausible values for the parameter
  • Accounts for sampling variability
  • Tells us both our estimate and how uncertain we are
\[\text{Interval Estimate} = \text{Statistic} \pm \text{Margin of Error}\]
Building Blocks

Margin of Error

How far from the statistic do we need to go to capture the parameter?

What Determines the Margin of Error?

  • The margin of error depends on the spread of the sampling distribution
  • Spread is measured by the standard error (SE)
  • A wider sampling distribution means we need a larger margin of error

The Formula

\[\text{Margin of Error} = z^* \times \text{SE}\]

For a 95% confidence interval with a bell-shaped sampling distribution:

\[\text{Margin of Error} \approx 2 \times \text{SE}\]
Intuition: About 95% of statistics fall within 2 standard errors of the parameter in a bell-shaped sampling distribution, so going 2 SE in each direction from our statistic should capture the parameter about 95% of the time.
Definition

What Is a Confidence Interval?

Formalizing our interval estimate with a confidence level.

Confidence Interval

  • An interval computed from sample data that captures the true population parameter for a specified proportion of all possible samples
  • The confidence level (e.g., 95%) tells us how often this method produces an interval that contains the truth
90%

90 out of 100 intervals computed this way will capture the true parameter

95%

95 out of 100 intervals computed this way will capture the true parameter

99%

99 out of 100 intervals computed this way will capture the true parameter

Computation

Computing a 95% Confidence Interval

Putting it all together with the standard error.

The 95% CI Formula

\[\text{95% CI} = \text{Statistic} \pm 2 \times \text{SE}\]

For a Mean

\[\bar{x} \pm 2 \times \text{SE}\]

Lower bound: \(\bar{x} - 2 \times \text{SE}\)

Upper bound: \(\bar{x} + 2 \times \text{SE}\)

For a Proportion

\[\hat{p} \pm 2 \times \text{SE}\]

Lower bound: \(\hat{p} - 2 \times \text{SE}\)

Upper bound: \(\hat{p} + 2 \times \text{SE}\)

Example: If \(\bar{x} = 68.5\) and \(\text{SE} = 1.2\), then the 95% CI is \(68.5 \pm 2(1.2) = (66.1,\; 70.9)\).
Interpretation

Interpreting a Confidence Interval

Getting the language exactly right matters.

Correct Interpretation Template

"We are 95% confident that the true [parameter] is between [lower bound] and [upper bound]."

Example: Mean

95% CI for mean height: (66.1, 70.9) inches

"We are 95% confident that the true mean height of the population is between 66.1 and 70.9 inches."

Example: Proportion

95% CI for proportion who favor: (0.52, 0.64)

"We are 95% confident that the true proportion of the population who favor the policy is between 0.52 and 0.64."

What does "95% confident" mean? If we repeated this sampling process many times, about 95% of the resulting intervals would contain the true parameter.
Caution

Common Misinterpretations

A 95% confidence interval does NOT mean any of the following.

WRONG Interpretation 1

"95% of the data in the population falls in this interval."

The CI is about the parameter, not individual data values. Data is much more spread out than a confidence interval.

WRONG Interpretation 2

"There is a 95% chance that the sample mean falls in this interval."

The sample mean is always in the center of the interval — it is not random once computed.

WRONG Interpretation 3

"The probability that the parameter is in this specific interval is 0.95."

The parameter is fixed. The interval either contains it or it doesn't. The 95% refers to the method, not this one interval.

The Core Issue

  • WE ONLY HAVE ONE SAMPLE
  • The confidence level describes what happens across many samples
  • For our one interval, we don't know if it's one of the 95% that captured the truth or the 5% that missed

Recap

Section 3.2 Summary

Key Formulas

\[\text{CI} = \text{Statistic} \pm \text{Margin of Error}\]
\[\text{95% CI} = \text{Statistic} \pm 2 \times \text{SE}\]

Margin of error is determined by the standard error (SE) of the sampling distribution.

Key Ideas

  • An interval estimate gives a range of plausible values for the parameter
  • The confidence level says how often the method captures the truth
  • "We are 95% confident the true [parameter] is between [lower] and [upper]"
  • Do not say "95% probability" about one specific interval
  • Do not confuse the CI with the range of data values
