What Is Statistics For?

Before a single formula, the goal for today: leave understanding what this subject is actually for — and why it is more interesting than its definition sounds.

A Definition — Really, Three Steps

Statistics is the branch of applied mathematics that deals with collecting, describing, and analyzing data.

1

Collect

Gather the right data — through surveys, experiments, or observation.
2

Describe

Summarize and visualize it — center, spread, patterns — so a human can hold it in their head.
3

Analyze

Draw conclusions and make decisions from the data.
We will do all three of these — in this very room — before the hour is out.

The Real Heart of the Subject

Statistics is fundamentally about making decisions and reasoning under uncertainty.

Why "under uncertainty"?

  • With perfect, complete information you would not need statistics — you would just look up the answer.
  • We can never measure everything.
  • We can never see the future.
  • The data we do have is noisy and incomplete.

Everyday statistical judgments

  • A doctor deciding whether a drug works.
  • A company deciding which product to launch.
  • You, deciding whether one bad review means a restaurant is actually bad.
Each one pulls a conclusion from incomplete evidence — and is honest about how confident you are allowed to be.

How We Fool Ourselves: Paul the Octopus

2010 World Cup. Two food boxes, each with a team's flag; whichever Paul ate from first was his "prediction." He got it right 8 times out of 8 — including the final.

The tempting reaction

  • "Is the octopus psychic?"
  • "Should we hire him to run the economy?"
How impressed should we actually be?

How a statistician thinks

Each guess is essentially a coin flip — two boxes, fifty-fifty. Eight in a row:
\( \left(\tfrac{1}{2}\right)^{8} = \dfrac{1}{256} \approx 0.39\% \)
Unlikely — but absolutely not impossible.
Now ask: how many animals worldwide made World Cup "predictions" that year? Dozens, maybe hundreds. If hundreds each take a shot, someone gets a lucky streak — and that one ends up on the news. Nobody films the octopus that got it wrong.

The Most Important Habit This Semester

When you see an impressive result, don't just ask how striking it is.

The instinct (weak question)

  • "How striking is this result?"
  • Treats a lucky streak as a talent.

The statistician (right question)

  • "How likely was something this striking to happen purely by chance?"
  • Separates real signal from lucky noise.
Paul had no skill. He had a coin and a good publicist. Distinguishing real signal from lucky noise is what we are here to learn.

The Other Source of Uncertainty: Sampling

We almost never get to measure everyone. Want the average age of every American? You won't reach all 330 million — you measure a sample and use it to estimate something about the whole population.

POPULATION → SAMPLE

Chosen well

  • A small sample can be remarkably accurate about a huge population.

Chosen badly

  • It lies to you confidently. Estimate the nation's age from only a college class and your answer skews young — biased.
  • No amount of clever math fixes a sample that was rotten from the start.

Let's Make It Real: How Old Is the Average American?

Our goal is one number — the average age of an American. We can't ask all 330 million, so we'll estimate it from a sample: us.

Collect

  • Everyone call out your age; we write them all on the board.
  • Each classmate is a case; the age we record is one variable — a numerical, or quantitative, one.
  • Our class is the sample we'll use to estimate the national average — not a census of the country.
  • That choice is exactly the issue we just discussed.

Our data — Summer 2026 class

Ages of the class
25, 24, 26, 35, 20, 21, 25, 36, 19, 19, 19, 40, 36, 19, 50, 20, 20, 17, 78, 17, 18, 27
The real ages from my Summer 2026 section — yours will look different each term.

Describe What We Collected

Summarize the numbers: where does the cluster sit, and how spread out is it?

Mean (average)

Add them all, divide by how many.
\( \bar{x} = \dfrac{\sum x}{n} \)
  • Summer 2026: 27.8 years — our estimate of the average American's age.

Median (middle)

Line them up in order; take the middle value.
  • Summer 2026: 22.5 years.
  • Lower than the mean — and that gap tells a story.

Spread

  • Ages run from 17 to 78 — a few older students stretch things out.
  • That one outlier at 78 yanks the mean up to 27.8, but the median barely moves from 22.5.
Right there, from your own ages, you see why statisticians don't trust a single number to describe a group.

Describe: A Picture of the Ages

A boxplot turns the whole class into one honest picture — center, spread, and anything unusual.

The middle half

  • Half the class falls between 19 and 35 — that range is the box.
  • The typical age (median) is about 22–23: half younger, half older.

Most common age

  • 19 shows up more than any other age — the mode.

An outlier

  • Ages stretch from 17 to 78, but 78 stands far apart from everyone else — a value well outside the usual range.
Because of that one high value, the average (about 28) comes out higher than the typical age of 22–23 — a perfect example of how a single extreme value pulls the mean up and away from what's "typical."

Analyze: Our Estimate vs. Reality

We set out to estimate the average age of an American. So — how close did our class get?

Our estimate

27.8
years — the average age in our Summer 2026 sample.

The real answer

38.2
years — the true average age of the U.S. population (2020 Census).
We missed by more than ten years. The math was fine — the sample was the problem. A college class isn't a random slice of America; it skews young, so our estimate does too. No amount of clever arithmetic fixes a sample that wasn't chosen honestly — the lesson at the heart of this whole course.
