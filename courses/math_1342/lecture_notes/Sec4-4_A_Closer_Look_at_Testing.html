Section 4.4

A Closer Look at Testing

Type I and Type II errors, multiple testing, publication bias, and the importance of replication.

Two Types of Errors

Hypothesis test conclusions are not perfect. Two kinds of mistakes are possible.

Reject \(H_0\)Do Not Reject \(H_0\)
\(H_0\) TrueType I ErrorCorrect
\(H_0\) FalseCorrectType II Error

In Plain English

  • Type I: Rejecting a true null. Finding a difference that is not really there.
  • Type II: Not rejecting a false null. Missing a real difference.

Probability of Type I Error

The significance level \(\alpha\) controls the chance of rejecting a true null.

Key Fact

\[ P(\text{Type I Error}) = \alpha \]
If \(\alpha = 0.05\), there is a 5% chance of rejecting \(H_0\) when it is actually true.

Adjusting \(\alpha\)

  • Decrease \(\alpha\) to reduce the chance of a Type I error.
  • Increase \(\alpha\) to reduce the chance of a Type II error.
  • There is always a trade-off between the two.

Multiple Testing

When many tests are performed, some will be significant just by chance.

The Problem

  • If 100 tests are done with \(\alpha = 0.05\) and nothing is really going on, about 5 will yield significant results just by chance.
  • The one significant result gets attention; the 95 non-significant ones are ignored.

Publication Bias

  • Usually only significant results get published.
  • No one knows about all the insignificant results.
  • This can create a very misleading picture of the evidence.

Sample Size and Significance

Larger samples make it easier to reject \(H_0\), for better or worse.

Effect of Sample Size

  • With small \(n\), even large effects may not reach significance (Type II errors are common).
  • With very large \(n\), even tiny, practically meaningless differences can be statistically significant.

Statistical vs. Practical Significance

A weight loss study with 10,000 people finds an average loss of 0.5 lbs is statistically significant. But is half a pound over a year practically meaningful?

Replication

Reproducing a study is the best guard against Type I errors and publication bias.

Replicated
Studies with similar results across settings gain credibility.
Contradicted
Studies with conflicting replications lose credibility.
Skepticism
Be skeptical when a claim is one of many tests. Look for replication.

What to Remember

Testing has limitations. Stay aware of them.

Errors

  • Type I: rejecting a true \(H_0\) (probability = \(\alpha\))
  • Type II: not rejecting a false \(H_0\)
  • Decrease \(\alpha\) to reduce Type I; increase \(n\) to reduce Type II

Big Picture

  • Multiple testing inflates false positives.
  • Publication bias hides non-significant results.
  • Replication is essential.
  • Statistical significance \(\neq\) practical significance.
