Section 1.2

Sampling from a Population

How we select a sample determines whether we can trust our conclusions about the population.

Sample vs. Population

We collect data on a sample and use it to make inferences about the larger population.

Population

  • Includes all individuals or objects of interest.
  • Usually too large to study completely.

Sample

  • A subset of the population.
  • The cases on which we actually collect data.
Statistical inference is the process of using data from a sample to gain information about the population.

Sampling Bias

When the sampling method causes the sample to differ from the population in a relevant way.

What Goes Wrong

  • The sample does not resemble the population.
  • Generalizations from the sample to the population cannot be trusted.
  • Even large samples can be biased.

Dewey Defeats Truman

In 1948, a large telephone poll predicted Dewey would win the election. Truman actually won.
The poll was biased because in 1948 phone ownership skewed toward wealthier voters who favored Dewey.

Random Sampling

The best way to avoid sampling bias is to take a random sample.

Simple Random Sample

  • Each unit in the population has the same chance of being selected.
  • Selection of one unit does not affect selection of another.
  • Like drawing names from a hat.

Why It Works

  • Random samples have statistics centered around the correct population value.
  • Non-random samples may suffer from bias.
  • Only random samples can truly be trusted for generalizations.

Bad Sampling Methods

These common approaches introduce bias and should be avoided.

Convenience
Sampling only people who are easy to reach, like students in the library when asking about study habits.
Volunteer
Letting people choose to participate. Those who respond are probably not representative.
Related
Sampling based on something obviously related to what you are studying.

Other Forms of Bias

Even with a random sample, data can still be biased.

Question Wording

How a question is phrased can dramatically change the results.
  • "Tax cut vs. new programs": 60% chose tax cut
  • "Tax cut vs. education, health, defense": 22% chose tax cut

Context

Surrounding information influences responses.
  • Ann Landers asked "Would you have children again?"
  • After a negative letter: 30% yes
  • After a concerned response: 95% yes

Inaccurate Responses

People may not answer truthfully, especially on sensitive topics.

What to Remember

Always think critically about how data were collected.

Key Ideas

  • We use sample data to make inferences about a population.
  • Sampling bias occurs when the sample does not resemble the population.
  • Random sampling avoids sampling bias.

Watch Out For

  • Convenience and volunteer samples.
  • Biased question wording or context.
  • Not all data collection leads to valid inferences.
