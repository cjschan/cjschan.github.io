Section 4.1

Introducing Hypothesis Tests

Using sample data to assess claims about a population

What Is a Statistical Test?

The core idea behind hypothesis testing

Definition

  • A statistical test uses data from a sample to assess a claim about a population
  • We collect evidence (data) and evaluate whether it supports or contradicts a specific claim
  • Similar to a courtroom trial: we start by assuming innocence and look for evidence of guilt

Key Ingredients

  • A claim about a population parameter
  • Sample data that can be compared to the claim
  • A method to measure how well data support or refute the claim
The goal of a statistical test is to determine whether the sample data provide enough evidence to refute a claim about a population.

Example: Can Dolphins Communicate?

A famous 1960s study by Dr. Jarvis Bastian asked: can dolphins communicate in an abstract manner? If Doris and Buzz cannot communicate, Buzz would just be guessing which button to push.

The Training

  • Two buttons were placed underwater at each end of a large pool
  • An old automobile headlight served as the signal: steady light = "push the button on the right," blinking light = "push the button on the left"
  • Pushing the correct button earned the dolphins a reward of fish — after months of training, Doris and Buzz got it right every time

The Real Study

  • A canvas curtain divided the pool: Doris could see the headlight, but Buzz could not
  • Buzz had to push the correct button, based only on Doris' whistles through the curtain
  • Dr. Bastian repeated the process many times, varying the light signal, and recorded how often Buzz pushed the correct button

Doris sees the light and must communicate to Buzz which button to push — Buzz cannot see the light from his side of the curtain.

The Dolphin Study: Data and Competing Claims

In one phase of the study, Buzz attempted to push the correct button a total of 16 times.

The Sample Data

  • Buzz pushed the correct button 15 out of 16 times
  • The sample statistic is \(\hat{p} = \frac{15}{16} = 0.9375\)
  • The parameter of interest is \(p\) = the true proportion of times Buzz pushes the correct button, in the long run

Two Possible Explanations

  • Buzz is just guessing (\(p = 0.50\)) and happened to get lucky in these 16 attempts
  • Buzz is doing something other than just guessing (\(p > 0.50\)) — the dolphins are communicating
Just guessing: \(p = 0.50\)     Communicating: \(p > 0.50\)
Think about it: Is 15 out of 16 correct pushes convincing to you? Or could Buzz have just been guessing and gotten lucky? How would you justify your answer?

Null and Alternative Hypotheses

The two competing statements in every hypothesis test

Null Hypothesis \(H_0\)

  • A statement that there is no effect or no difference
  • Represents the "status quo" or default position
  • Usually contains an equality sign: \(=\)
  • We assume \(H_0\) is true and look for evidence against it

Alternative Hypothesis \(H_a\)

  • The claim we are seeking evidence for
  • Represents a new effect or a difference from the status quo
  • Usually contains an inequality: \(>\), \(<\), or \(\neq\)
  • We need convincing evidence to support \(H_a\)
Key point: The null hypothesis is the "default" that we assume true. The alternative hypothesis is what we hope to find evidence for. A hypothesis test asks: "Is the data surprising enough to reject \(H_0\)?"

Writing Hypotheses

How to set up the null and alternative for a given scenario

Dolphin Communication Example

Claim: Buzz pushes the correct button more often than he would by random guessing.

\(H_0: p = 0.50\)
\(H_a: p > 0.50\)

Here \(p\) = the true proportion of times Buzz pushes the correct button. The sample gives \(\hat{p} = 15/16 \approx 0.94\), but the hypotheses are about \(p\), not \(\hat{p}\).

Tips for Writing Hypotheses

  • Hypotheses are always about population parameters (not sample statistics)
  • Use notation like \(p\), \(\mu\), \(\mu_1 - \mu_2\), etc.
  • \(H_0\) always includes the "=" case
  • \(H_a\) is determined by the research question
  • Set up hypotheses before collecting data

Example: Sleep vs. Caffeine

Does sleep or caffeine have a different effect on memory performance?

The Study

  • Researchers randomly assign subjects to either take a nap or drink caffeine
  • Later, both groups complete a memory task
  • We want to know if there is a difference in mean scores between the two groups

Setting Up Hypotheses

Let \(\mu_s\) = mean score for the sleep group
Let \(\mu_c\) = mean score for the caffeine group

\(H_0: \mu_s = \mu_c\)
\(H_a: \mu_s \neq \mu_c\)

This is a two-sided test because we are looking for any difference, not a specific direction.

One-Sided vs. Two-Sided Tests

The direction of the alternative hypothesis matters

Right-Tailed (Upper)

\(H_0: \text{parameter} = \text{value}\)
\(H_a: \text{parameter} > \text{value}\)

Evidence that the parameter is greater than the claimed value.

Example: Dolphin communication

Left-Tailed (Lower)

\(H_0: \text{parameter} = \text{value}\)
\(H_a: \text{parameter} < \text{value}\)

Evidence that the parameter is less than the claimed value.

Example: Drug lowers blood pressure

Two-Tailed

\(H_0: \text{parameter} = \text{value}\)
\(H_a: \text{parameter} \neq \text{value}\)

Evidence that the parameter differs from the claimed value in either direction.

Example: Sleep vs. Caffeine

One-sided tests look for evidence in a specific direction. Two-sided tests look for evidence of any difference from the null value.

What If Buzz Were Just Guessing?

Suppose \(H_0: p = 0.50\) is true. Below: 1000 simulated sample proportions \(\hat{p}\) from samples of \(n = 16\) attempts, assuming Buzz is just guessing. Buzz's actual result, \(\hat{p} = 15/16 \approx 0.94\), lies far out in the tail — it essentially never happens by random chance alone.

Summary: Introducing Hypothesis Tests

Key takeaways from Section 4.1

Core Concepts

  • A statistical test uses sample data to assess a claim about a population
  • The null hypothesis \(H_0\) states "no effect" or "no difference"
  • The alternative hypothesis \(H_a\) is the claim we seek evidence for
  • Hypotheses are always about population parameters

Important Details

  • \(H_0\) always includes \(=\); \(H_a\) uses \(>\), \(<\), or \(\neq\)
  • The alternative can be one-sided (\(>\) or \(<\)) or two-sided (\(\neq\))
  • We assume \(H_0\) is true and look for evidence against it
  • The goal is to determine if data provide enough evidence to reject \(H_0\) in favor of \(H_a\)
