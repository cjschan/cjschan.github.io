Significance

Using p-values to make formal decisions about hypotheses

Strength of Evidence

Smaller p-values provide stronger evidence against \(H_0\)

Informal Guidelines for p-value Strength

p-valueStrength of Evidence Against \(H_0\)
Greater than 0.1Not enough evidence
0.05 to 0.1Some evidence
0.01 to 0.05Moderate evidence
Less than 0.01Strong evidence

Small p-value

  • The observed result is unlikely under \(H_0\)
  • The data are surprising if \(H_0\) is true
  • We have evidence against \(H_0\)

Large p-value

  • The observed result is plausible under \(H_0\)
  • The data are not surprising if \(H_0\) is true
  • We do not have evidence against \(H_0\)

The Significance Level \(\alpha\)

Setting a threshold for "small enough"

Definition

  • The significance level \(\alpha\) (alpha) is a pre-chosen threshold for the p-value
  • It is the boundary between "convincing" and "not convincing" evidence
  • The most common choice is \(\alpha = 0.05\)
  • Other common values: \(\alpha = 0.01\) or \(\alpha = 0.10\)

Why 0.05?

  • Convention established by R.A. Fisher in the early 1900s
  • Means we accept a 5% risk of rejecting \(H_0\) when it is actually true
  • Balances being too lenient vs. too strict
  • The choice of \(\alpha\) should be made before looking at data
The significance level \(\alpha\) is the threshold: if the p-value falls below \(\alpha\), we consider the evidence strong enough to reject \(H_0\).

Making Formal Decisions

The two possible outcomes of a hypothesis test

If p-value \(< \alpha\)

Reject \(H_0\)
  • The results are statistically significant
  • We have enough evidence to conclude \(H_a\)
  • The observed result is unlikely to have occurred by random chance alone

If p-value \(\geq \alpha\)

Do Not Reject \(H_0\)
  • The results are not statistically significant
  • We do not have enough evidence to conclude \(H_a\)
  • The observed result is plausible under random chance
Remember: We always set \(\alpha\) before collecting data, not after. Choosing \(\alpha\) after seeing the p-value would be cheating!

Statistical Significance

Applying the decision rule to real examples

Definition

A result is statistically significant if the p-value is less than the significance level \(\alpha\).
Example 1

Resveratrol Study A

p-value = 0.04, \(\alpha = 0.05\)

Since \(0.04 < 0.05\):

Reject \(H_0\) -- Significant

Example 2

Resveratrol Study B

p-value = 0.12, \(\alpha = 0.05\)

Since \(0.12 \geq 0.05\):

Do not reject \(H_0\) -- Not significant

Example 3

Resveratrol Study C

p-value = 0.0003, \(\alpha = 0.05\)

Since \(0.0003 < 0.05\):

Reject \(H_0\) -- Highly significant

Never "Accept" \(H_0\)

An important distinction in hypothesis testing language

Common Mistake

WRONG: "We accept \(H_0\)"
  • Failing to reject \(H_0\) does not mean \(H_0\) is true
  • It only means we did not find enough evidence against it
  • Like a jury verdict of "not guilty" -- it does not mean "innocent"

Correct Language

CORRECT: "We do not reject \(H_0\)"
  • Lack of evidence against \(H_0\) is not the same as evidence for \(H_0\)
  • "We do not have sufficient evidence to conclude \(H_a\)"
  • The study may lack power (e.g., sample too small) to detect a real effect
Analogy: "Absence of evidence is not evidence of absence." Just because we cannot prove something, it does not mean it is false.

Conclusions in Context

How to write a proper conclusion for a hypothesis test

Conclusion Template

Step 1: State the decision (reject or do not reject \(H_0\)).
Step 2: State the conclusion in context about \(H_a\).

When We Reject \(H_0\)

Example: Testing whether resveratrol helps with weight loss. p-value = 0.04, \(\alpha = 0.05\).

"We reject \(H_0\). There is sufficient evidence that resveratrol is associated with weight loss."

When We Do Not Reject \(H_0\)

Example: Testing whether a coin is unfair. p-value = 0.35, \(\alpha = 0.05\).

"We do not reject \(H_0\). There is not sufficient evidence that the coin is unfair."

Always write conclusions in the context of the original problem. Avoid generic statements like "reject \(H_0\)" without connecting back to the real-world question.

Summary: Determining Statistical Significance

Key takeaways from Section 4.3

Decision Framework

  • The significance level \(\alpha\) is the cutoff (commonly 0.05)
  • p-value \(< \alpha\) → Reject \(H_0\) (statistically significant)
  • p-value \(\geq \alpha\) → Do not reject \(H_0\) (not significant)
  • Smaller p-value = stronger evidence against \(H_0\)

Important Reminders

  • Never say "accept \(H_0\)" -- only "do not reject"
  • Lack of evidence against \(H_0 \neq\) evidence for \(H_0\)
  • Always state conclusions in context
  • The informal scale (0.1, 0.05, 0.01) provides useful guidance beyond strict cutoffs
The big picture: Hypothesis testing gives us a principled framework for using data to assess claims. The p-value quantifies evidence, and the significance level \(\alpha\) provides a decision boundary.
