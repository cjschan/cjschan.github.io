Section 6.5

Paired Difference in Means

Use paired data by turning each pair into one difference, then do one-sample t inference on those differences.

What Counts as Paired Data?

Paired data arise when the two measurements in a comparison naturally belong together.

Common Examples

  • Two measurements on the same person or object.
  • Matched-pairs experiments.
  • Twin studies or carefully matched cases.

Main Idea

  • Do not analyze the two columns as independent samples.
  • Compute one difference for each pair.
  • Then do inference on the mean of those differences.

Paired vs Separate Samples

The distinction depends on whether each observation in one group is directly linked to one observation in the other group.

Before / After
Same person measured twice. This is paired data.
Twins
Each twin is matched with the other. This is paired data.
Two groups
If different people receive different treatments with no pairing, use separate-sample methods instead.

Analyze the Differences

For paired data, build a new variable \(d\) and reduce the problem to one-sample inference for a mean.

Create a Difference

\[ d = \text{response 1} - \text{response 2} \]
  • Compute one difference for each matched pair.
  • The sign depends on the order you choose.

Then Use One-Mean t Methods

\[ SE(\bar{d})=\frac{s_d}{\sqrt{n_d}}, \qquad df=n_d-1 \]
  • If the differences are approximately normal, or \(n_d \ge 30\), use a t distribution.
  • Everything now depends on the differences, not the original two columns.

Pheromones in Tears Setup

Each of 50 men was tested twice: once with real female tears and once with a salt solution. That makes this paired data.

Summary of Differences

  • Define \(d=\) testosterone with tears minus testosterone with no tears.
  • Sample mean of differences: \(\bar{d}=-21.7\) pg/ml
  • Sample SD of differences: \(s_d=46.5\)
  • Number of pairs: \(n_d=50\)

Question

  • Do female tears lower male testosterone levels?
  • Because \(\bar{d}\) is negative, lower testosterone corresponds to \(\mu_d<0\).

Pheromones in Tears Hypothesis Test

Use a one-sample t test on the differences.

Step 1
\[ H_0:\mu_d=0 \qquad H_a:\mu_d<0 \]
Step 2
\[ SE=\frac{46.5}{\sqrt{50}}\approx 6.58 \]
With \(n_d=50\), the sample size is large enough for t procedures.
Step 3
\[ t=\frac{-21.7-0}{6.58}\approx -3.30 \]
Step 4
\[ df=50-1=49,\qquad \text{p-value}\approx 0.0009 \]
Conclusion: There is very strong evidence that exposure to female tears lowers male testosterone levels on average.

Pheromones in Tears Confidence Interval

The same paired differences can also be used to estimate the average effect size.

Step 1
\[ df=49,\qquad t^*\approx 2.01 \text{ for a } 95\% \text{ CI} \]
Step 2
\[ SE=\frac{46.5}{\sqrt{50}}\approx 6.58 \]
Step 3
\[ ME=2.01(6.58)\approx 13.22 \]
Step 4
\[ -21.7 \pm 13.22 = (-34.92,\ -8.48) \]
Interpretation: We are \(95\%\) confident that female tears lower testosterone by about \(8.5\) to \(34.9\) pg/ml on average.

Why Use Paired Data?

Pairing often reduces variability by comparing each unit to itself, or to a very similar unit.

Lower SD
Differences can vary less than the original responses.
Lower SE
A smaller standard deviation of differences leads to more precise inference.
More Power
Reducing noise makes it easier to detect real effects.

What to Remember

Paired-data inference is really one-sample mean inference applied to the differences.

Confidence Interval

\[ \bar{d}\pm t^* \cdot \frac{s_d}{\sqrt{n_d}} \]
  • Compute differences first.
  • Use \(df=n_d-1\).

Hypothesis Test

\[ t=\frac{\bar{d}-\mu_{d,0}}{s_d/\sqrt{n_d}} \]
  • Need \(n_d \ge 30\) or approximately normal differences.
  • Do not treat paired data as two independent samples.
