Inference for a Difference in Proportions

Confidence intervals and hypothesis tests for comparing two population proportions.

\[ (\hat{p}_1-\hat{p}_2)\pm z^* \cdot SE \]

One Proportion or Two?

The key question is whether we are describing one categorical variable or comparing proportions across two groups.

One Proportion

  • There is only one categorical variable.
  • Example: proportion of students at a university who are in-state.
  • We estimate or test a single population proportion \(p\).

Two Proportions

  • There are two groups being compared on a categorical outcome.
  • Example: compare financial-aid rates for in-state and out-of-state students.
  • Now the parameter is the difference \(p_1-p_2\).

Standard Error and CLT

For large enough samples, the difference in sample proportions is approximately normal.

Standard Error

\[ SE(\hat{p}_1-\hat{p}_2)= \sqrt{\frac{p_1(1-p_1)}{n_1}+\frac{p_2(1-p_2)}{n_2}} \]
This is the population version. In practice, the values used inside the SE depend on whether we are building a confidence interval or doing a hypothesis test.

Large-Sample Condition

  • Check counts in each cell of the two-way table.
  • If each cell count is at least \(10\), then \(\hat{p}_1-\hat{p}_2\) is approximately normal.
  • That lets us use z-based confidence intervals and z tests.

Confidence Interval for \(p_1-p_2\)

For a confidence interval, use the sample proportions as the best estimates of the population proportions.

CI Formula

\[ (\hat{p}_1-\hat{p}_2)\pm z^* \sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}_1(1-\hat{p}_1)}{n_1}+\frac{\hat{p}_2(1-\hat{p}_2)}{n_2}} \]
For a confidence interval, replace \(p_1\) and \(p_2\) with \(\hat{p}_1\) and \(\hat{p}_2\).

Interpretation

  • The interval gives plausible values for the true difference \(p_1-p_2\).
  • If the interval stays far from \(0\), the groups appear meaningfully different.
  • The sign tells the direction of the difference.

Penguins Confidence Interval Example

A study compared 10-year survival rates for penguins with metal tags and electronic tags.

Setup
  • Group 1: metal tag, \(n_1=167\), \(\hat{p}_1=0.20\)
  • Group 2: electronic tag, \(n_2=189\), \(\hat{p}_2=0.36\)
  • We want a \(90\%\) confidence interval for \(p_1-p_2\).
Step 1
\[ \hat{p}_1-\hat{p}_2=0.20-0.36=-0.16 \]
Step 2
\[ SE \approx \sqrt{\frac{0.20(0.80)}{167}+\frac{0.36(0.64)}{189}} \approx 0.0467 \]
Step 3
\[ -0.16 \pm 1.645(0.0467) = -0.16 \pm 0.0768 \]
Result
\[ (-0.237,\ -0.083) \]
Interpretation: We are \(90\%\) confident that the survival rate is between \(8.3\%\) and \(23.7\%\) lower for metal-tagged penguins than for electronically tagged penguins.

Hypothesis Testing Uses a Pooled Proportion

Under the null hypothesis of no difference, both groups are treated as having the same population proportion.

Pooled Proportion

\[ \hat{p}=\frac{x_1+x_2}{n_1+n_2} \]
This is the overall sample proportion across both groups combined.
  • \(\hat{p}\) will lie between the two observed sample proportions.
  • Use it only for the hypothesis test, not for the confidence interval.

z Test Statistic

\[ z=\frac{(\hat{p}_1-\hat{p}_2)-0} {\sqrt{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})\left(\frac{1}{n_1}+\frac{1}{n_2}\right)}} \]
The p-value is the area in the tail(s) beyond the observed \(z\) in a standard normal distribution.

Penguins Hypothesis Test

The same penguin study can be framed as a test of whether metal tags reduce survival.

Step 1
\[ H_0:p_1-p_2=0 \qquad H_a:p_1-p_2<0 \]
Here, Group 1 is metal tagged and Group 2 is electronically tagged.
Step 2
\[ \hat{p}=\frac{33+68}{167+189}\approx 0.284 \]
Step 3
\[ z \approx \frac{0.198-0.360}{\sqrt{0.284(0.716)\left(\frac{1}{167}+\frac{1}{189}\right)}} \approx -3.39 \]
Step 4
The left-tail p-value is very small, so there is very strong evidence that metal tags are detrimental to penguins.

Treatment vs Placebo Practice

A randomized double-blind experiment compared pain relief rates for a treatment and a placebo.

Two-Way Table

Treatment Placebo Total
Relieved pain 36 21 57
Did not relieve pain 39 54 93
Total 75 75 150
Pooled Proportion
\[ \hat{p}=\frac{57}{150}=0.38 \]
Test Statistic
\[ z=\frac{0.48-0.28}{\sqrt{0.38(0.62)\left(\frac{1}{75}+\frac{1}{75}\right)}} \approx 2.52 \]
p-Value
\[ \text{p-value}\approx 0.0059 \]

Formula Method vs Simulation

Both approaches target the same sampling logic, but their accuracy depends on different things.

Simulation
Bootstrap and randomization results improve when you run more simulations.
Formulas
Normal-approximation intervals and p-values improve as the sample sizes get larger.
Match
If the sampling distribution is close to normal and the simulation uses many repetitions, the answers should be very close.
Bootstrap CI
The bootstrap dot plot shows the simulated distribution used to form the confidence interval for the difference in proportions.

What to Remember

The procedure changes depending on whether you are estimating a difference or testing for one.

Confidence Interval

\[ (\hat{p}_1-\hat{p}_2)\pm z^* \sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}_1(1-\hat{p}_1)}{n_1}+\frac{\hat{p}_2(1-\hat{p}_2)}{n_2}} \]
  • Use \(\hat{p}_1\) and \(\hat{p}_2\) in the SE formula.
  • Interpret the interval as a plausible range for \(p_1-p_2\).
  • Check that each cell count is at least \(10\).

Hypothesis Test

\[ z=\frac{(\hat{p}_1-\hat{p}_2)-0} {\sqrt{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})\left(\frac{1}{n_1}+\frac{1}{n_2}\right)}} \]
  • Use the pooled proportion \(\hat{p}\).
  • The p-value is the tail area beyond the observed \(z\).
  • For this section, the null value is usually \(p_1-p_2=0\).
