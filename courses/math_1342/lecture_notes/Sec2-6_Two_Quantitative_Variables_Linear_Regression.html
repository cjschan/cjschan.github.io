Section 2.6

Linear Regression

Fitting a line to predict one quantitative variable from another.

What Is Regression?

Linear regression finds the straight line that best fits data in a scatterplot.

Goal

  • Find a line to predict the response variable \(y\) from the explanatory variable \(x\).
  • The line of best fit is called the regression line.

Example: Crickets and Temperature

Can you estimate the temperature by listening to crickets chirp?
\[ \widehat{\text{Temp}} = 37.7 + 0.23 \times \text{Chirps} \]

A Quick Review: Graphing Lines

A regression line is just a line — the same \(y = mx + b\) you met in algebra.

Slope–Intercept Form

\[ y = mx + b \]
  • \(b\) is the \(y\)-intercept: the height where the line crosses the \(y\)-axis (when \(x = 0\)).
  • \(m\) is the slope: \(\dfrac{\text{rise}}{\text{run}} = \dfrac{\text{change in } y}{\text{change in } x}\).
  • To graph: start at \((0, b)\), then use the slope to step to the next point.

Example: \(y = 1 + 2x\)

Intercept \(b = 1\) (crosses \(y\)-axis at 1). Slope \(m = 2\): up 2 for every 1 step right.

Slope and Intercept

The two numbers that define the regression line.

Slope \(b_1\)

  • The increase in predicted \(y\) for every one-unit increase in \(x\).
  • Example: temperature goes up by about \(0.23^\circ\text{F}\) for each additional chirp per minute.

Intercept \(b_0\)

  • The predicted \(y\) value when \(x = 0\).
  • Often not meaningful if \(x = 0\) is far outside the data range.

Interpreting Slope & Intercept in Context

Don't just report the numbers — say what they mean, and attach the right units. Recall \(\widehat{\text{Temp}} = 37.7 + 0.23 \times \text{Chirps}\).

Slope: \(b_1 = 0.23\)

  • For each additional 1 chirp per minute, the predicted temperature goes up by about \(0.23^\circ\text{F}\).
  • Units of slope \(= \dfrac{\text{units of } y}{\text{units of } x} = \dfrac{^\circ\text{F}}{\text{chirp/min}}\).

Intercept: \(b_0 = 37.7\)

  • When chirps \(= 0\), the predicted temperature is \(37.7^\circ\text{F}\).
  • Units of intercept \(=\) units of \(y = {}^\circ\text{F}\).
  • Interpret with care: \(0\) chirps may be outside the data, so this is just where the line starts.
Units rule of thumb: the slope is measured in \(y\)-units per \(x\)-unit, and the intercept is measured in \(y\)-units.

Making Predictions

Plug an x-value into the equation to get a predicted y-value.

Example
If crickets chirp 140 times per minute:
\[ \hat{y} = 37.7 + 0.23(140) = 69.9^\circ\text{F} \]
Another
If crickets chirp 180 times per minute:
\[ \hat{y} = 37.7 + 0.23(180) = 79.1^\circ\text{F} \]

Residuals

A residual measures how far the observed value is from the predicted value.

Definition

\[ \text{Residual} = y - \hat{y} = \text{observed} - \text{predicted} \]
Geometrically, it is the vertical distance from the point to the line.

Least Squares Line

  • The regression line minimizes the sum of squared residuals.
  • This is why it is called the least squares line.
  • We rely on technology to find it.

Example: Computing a Residual

Using \(\widehat{\text{Temp}} = 37.7 + 0.23 \times \text{Chirps}\). Suppose crickets chirp 140 times per minute and the actual temperature is \(72^\circ\text{F}\).

Step 1 — Predict

\[ \hat{y} = 37.7 + 0.23(140) = 69.9^\circ\text{F} \]

Step 2 — Subtract

\[ y - \hat{y} = 72 - 69.9 = 2.1^\circ\text{F} \]

What It Means

  • The residual is \(+2.1^\circ\text{F}\).
  • It is positive, so the observed point sits above the line.
  • The model under-predicted: the real day was \(2.1^\circ\text{F}\) warmer than the line said.
  • Units of a residual match \(y\): degrees Fahrenheit.
A negative residual would mean the point is below the line and the model over-predicted.

Regression Cautions

Four important warnings when using regression.

Do Not Extrapolate

  • Do not predict far beyond the range of your data.
  • If \(x = 0\) is not near your data, the intercept is meaningless.

Check Your Assumptions

  • Only use the line if the relationship is approximately linear.
  • Outliers can be very influential on the regression line.
  • It matters which variable is explanatory and which is response.
Always plot your data! Technology will compute a regression line for any two quantitative variables, even if the relationship is not linear.

The Scenario

A dealership studies how a used car's age (in years) relates to its price (in dollars). The least-squares regression line is:
\[ \widehat{\text{Price}} = 21{,}000 - 1{,}500 \times \text{Age} \]
The data used to fit this line came from cars between 1 and 12 years old.

Your Tasks

  • (a) Interpret the slope in context, including units.
  • (b) Interpret the intercept in context, including units.
  • (c) Predict the price of a car that is 6 years old.
  • (d) One 6-year-old car actually sold for $13,500. Find the residual and explain what it tells you.
  • (e) Should you use this line to predict the price of a 25-year-old car? Why or why not?

What to Remember

Regression gives a prediction equation; use it wisely.

Core Ideas

  • \(\hat{y} = b_0 + b_1 x\)
  • Slope: change in \(\hat{y}\) per unit change in \(x\)
  • Residual: \(y - \hat{y}\)
  • Least squares minimizes \(\sum (\text{residuals})^2\)

Cautions

  • Do not extrapolate.
  • Always plot your data.
  • Watch for influential outliers.
  • Correlation and regression do not prove causation.
