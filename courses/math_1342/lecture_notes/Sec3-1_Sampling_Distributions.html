Section 3.1

Sampling Distributions

Understanding how sample statistics behave across repeated sampling

Big Picture

Statistical Inference

The core goal of statistics: learn about populations from samples.

The Problem

  • We want to know about an entire population
  • It is usually impossible or impractical to measure everyone
  • So we collect a sample and use it to draw conclusions

Statistical Inference

  • Drawing conclusions about a population based on information from a sample
  • We use sample data to estimate, predict, and make decisions about the population
Key idea: We never know the true population values exactly — we use samples to get our best estimates.
Definitions

Parameter vs Statistic

Two categories of numerical summaries — one for populations, one for samples.

Parameter (Population)

A number that describes the population. Usually unknown.

\(\mu\) = population mean
\(\sigma\) = population std dev
\(p\) = population proportion
\(\rho\) = population correlation

Statistic (Sample)

A number computed from a sample. Known and calculable.

\(\bar{x}\) = sample mean
\(s\) = sample std dev
\(\hat{p}\) = sample proportion
\(r\) = sample correlation
Remember: Parameters are fixed but unknown. Statistics are known but vary from sample to sample.
Estimation

Point Estimates

Using a single sample statistic to estimate a population parameter.

What Is a Point Estimate?

  • A point estimate is the value of a sample statistic used to estimate a population parameter
  • It is our single "best guess" for the parameter
  • Example: Use \(\bar{x}\) to estimate \(\mu\)
  • Example: Use \(\hat{p}\) to estimate \(p\)

The Catch

  • A point estimate is almost certainly not exactly equal to the parameter
  • Different samples give different statistics
  • Sample statistics vary from sample to sample
  • This natural variability is called sampling variability
Question: If every sample gives a slightly different answer, how do we know how close our estimate is to the truth?
Key Concept

Sampling Distribution

What happens when we look at statistics from many, many samples?

Definition

  • A sampling distribution is the distribution of a sample statistic computed from many different samples of the same size drawn from the same population

Step 1

Take a random sample of size \(n\) from the population

Step 2

Compute the statistic (e.g., \(\bar{x}\) or \(\hat{p}\))

Step 3

Repeat many times and look at the distribution of all those statistics

Think of it this way: The sampling distribution tells us how much a statistic tends to vary across different samples.
Properties

Center and Shape of the Sampling Distribution

Two important features that emerge when we use random samples.

Center

  • If we take random samples, the sampling distribution is centered around the population parameter
  • The mean of all sample means \(= \mu\)
  • The mean of all sample proportions \(= p\)
  • This means sample statistics are unbiased estimators of the parameter

Shape

  • For large enough sample size \(n\), the sampling distribution is approximately symmetric and bell-shaped
  • This holds regardless of the shape of the original population
  • This remarkable result is connected to the Central Limit Theorem
Key takeaway: Random sampling guarantees the sampling distribution is centered at the truth, and large samples make it bell-shaped.
Spread

Standard Error (SE)

Measuring how much a sample statistic varies from sample to sample.

Definition

  • The standard error (SE) is the standard deviation of the sampling distribution
  • It measures the typical distance between a sample statistic and the population parameter
\[\text{SE} = \text{Std Dev of Sampling Distribution}\]

Why It Matters

  • Small SE means statistics cluster tightly around the parameter — more precise
  • Large SE means statistics spread widely — less precise
  • SE tells us how much "error" we typically expect in our estimate
Note: Standard error is NOT the same as standard deviation. SD describes variability in individual data values; SE describes variability in a statistic.
Important Relationship

Sample Size and Standard Error

How does increasing \(n\) affect the precision of our estimates?

Small \(n\)

Large SE

Statistics vary a lot from sample to sample. Estimates are less precise.

Medium \(n\)

Medium SE

Moderate variability. Estimates are reasonably precise.

Large \(n\)

Small SE

Statistics cluster tightly around the parameter. Estimates are very precise.

The rule: Larger sample size \(\Rightarrow\) smaller standard error \(\Rightarrow\) less variability \(\Rightarrow\) more precise estimates.
Recap

Section 3.1 Summary

Core Concepts

  • Statistical inference uses sample data to draw conclusions about populations
  • Parameters describe populations; statistics describe samples
  • A point estimate is a sample statistic used to estimate a parameter
  • Sample statistics vary from sample to sample

Sampling Distribution

  • The distribution of a statistic from many samples of the same size
  • Center: at the population parameter (for random samples)
  • Shape: symmetric and bell-shaped for large \(n\)
  • Spread: measured by the standard error (SE)
  • Larger \(n\) gives smaller SE and more precise estimates
