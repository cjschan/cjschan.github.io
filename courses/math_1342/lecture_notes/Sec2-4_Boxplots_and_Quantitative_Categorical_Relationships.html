Section 2.4

Boxplots and Quantitative/Categorical Relationships

Identifying outliers, constructing boxplots, and comparing quantitative data across groups.

Identifying Outliers

A rule of thumb uses the IQR to flag unusual values.

Outlier Rule

A data value is an outlier if it is:
\[ \text{smaller than } Q_1 - 1.5 \times \text{IQR} \]
\[ \text{or larger than } Q_3 + 1.5 \times \text{IQR} \]

Example

Five number summary: (2, 15, 46, 149, 536)
  • IQR = 149 - 15 = 134
  • Lower fence: 15 - 1.5(134) = -186
  • Upper fence: 149 + 1.5(134) = 350
  • Values above 350 are outliers: 402, 447, 511, 536

Constructing a Boxplot

A boxplot displays the five number summary and flags outliers.

Steps

  • Draw a box from \(Q_1\) to \(Q_3\).
  • Draw a line inside the box at the median.
  • Extend whiskers to the most extreme non-outlier values.
  • Plot each outlier individually with a dot or asterisk.

What You Can Read

  • Center: the median line.
  • Spread: width of the box (IQR).
  • Shape: compare whisker lengths for skewness.
  • Outliers: individual points beyond whiskers.

Quantitative and Categorical Relationships

Break down a quantitative variable by categorical groups to compare distributions.

Side-by-Side Boxplots

  • One boxplot for each category on the same scale.
  • Compare centers, spreads, and outliers across groups.

Difference in Means

\[ \bar{x}_1 - \bar{x}_2 \]
The summary statistic for comparing a quantitative response across two groups.

What to Remember

Boxplots and side-by-side comparisons complete the descriptive statistics toolkit.

One Quantitative Variable

  • Visualizations: dotplot, histogram, boxplot
  • Center: mean, median
  • Spread: SD, range, IQR
  • Outliers: 1.5 × IQR rule

Quantitative + Categorical

  • Visualization: side-by-side boxplots
  • Statistics by group
  • Summary: difference in means
