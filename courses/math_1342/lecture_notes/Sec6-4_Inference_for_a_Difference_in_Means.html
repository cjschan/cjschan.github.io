Section 6.4

Inference for a Difference in Means

Confidence intervals and hypothesis tests for comparing two population means from separate samples.

\[ (\bar{x}_1-\bar{x}_2)\pm t^* \cdot SE \]

Standard Error and Degrees of Freedom

For two-sample means, estimate each population standard deviation with its sample standard deviation.

Standard Error

\[ SE(\bar{x}_1-\bar{x}_2)=\sqrt{\frac{s_1^2}{n_1}+\frac{s_2^2}{n_2}} \]
We do not know \(\sigma_1\) or \(\sigma_2\), so we use \(s_1\) and \(s_2\).

t Distribution

\[ df=\min(n_1-1,\ n_2-1) \]
  • Use the smaller sample size to set the degrees of freedom.
  • Need each sample to be large, or each population distribution to be approximately normal.

Confidence Interval for \(\mu_1-\mu_2\)

The two-sample confidence interval has the same structure as before, but the SE now includes both samples.

\[ (\bar{x}_1-\bar{x}_2)\pm t^* \sqrt{\frac{s_1^2}{n_1}+\frac{s_2^2}{n_2}} \]
  • \(t^*\) comes from a t-distribution with \(df=\min(n_1-1,n_2-1)\).
  • The sign depends on the order of Group 1 and Group 2.

Conditions

  • Both samples should come from random assignment or random sampling.
  • Use t procedures when both samples are large, or when both distributions are reasonably normal.
  • Smaller samples require more caution if the data are skewed.

Video Games and GPA Setup

Students were randomly assigned roommates to study whether a roommate bringing a video game system affects GPA.

Group Summary

Group\(n\)Mean GPASD
Roommate brought system782.840.669
Roommate did not bring system1323.1050.625

Question

  • Let Group 1 = videogame system and Group 2 = no videogame system.
  • We want a \(90\%\) confidence interval for \(\mu_1-\mu_2\).
  • If the interval is negative, GPA is lower in the videogame-roommate group.

Video Games and GPA Confidence Interval

Work through the two-sample t interval step by step.

Step 1
\[ df=\min(78-1,\ 132-1)=77 \]
Step 2
\[ t^* \approx 1.665 \text{ for a } 90\% \text{ CI} \]
Step 3
\[ \bar{x}_1-\bar{x}_2=2.84-3.105=-0.265 \]
Step 4
\[ SE=\sqrt{\frac{0.669^2}{78}+\frac{0.625^2}{132}}\approx 0.093 \]
Step 5
\[ -0.265 \pm 1.665(0.093)= -0.265 \pm 0.155 \]
\[ (-0.420,\ -0.110) \]
Interpretation: We are \(90\%\) confident that having a roommate who brings a videogame system lowers first-semester GPA by about \(0.11\) to \(0.42\) points on average.

Two-Sample t Test

Hypothesis testing compares the observed difference in sample means to the null value of \(0\).

Test Statistic

\[ t=\frac{(\bar{x}_1-\bar{x}_2)-0}{\sqrt{\frac{s_1^2}{n_1}+\frac{s_2^2}{n_2}}} \]
Use a t-distribution with \(df=\min(n_1-1,n_2-1)\).

Hypotheses

\[ H_0:\mu_1-\mu_2=0 \qquad H_a:\mu_1-\mu_2 \neq 0 \]
  • Tail direction depends on the research question.
  • The p-value is the area beyond the observed \(t\) in the t distribution.

Pygmalion Effect Example

Teachers were told some randomly selected students were “growth spurters.” The response was change in IQ over one year.

Group\(n\)Mean changeSD
Growth spurters6512.2213.3
Control students2558.4212.0
Research question: does expecting a child to do well actually cause the child to do better?
Hypotheses
\[ H_0:\mu_1-\mu_2=0 \qquad H_a:\mu_1-\mu_2>0 \]
Computation
\[ SE=\sqrt{\frac{13.3^2}{65}+\frac{12.0^2}{255}}\approx 1.813 \]
\[ t=\frac{12.22-8.42}{1.813}\approx 2.10 \]
With \(df=64\), the right-tail p-value is about \(0.020\). That gives statistically significant evidence that the expected “growth spurters” improved more on average.

What to Remember

Difference-in-means inference extends the one-mean t methods to two separate samples.

Confidence Interval

\[ (\bar{x}_1-\bar{x}_2)\pm t^* \sqrt{\frac{s_1^2}{n_1}+\frac{s_2^2}{n_2}} \]
  • Use separate sample standard deviations.
  • Interpret the interval as a plausible range for \(\mu_1-\mu_2\).

Hypothesis Test

\[ t=\frac{(\bar{x}_1-\bar{x}_2)-0}{\sqrt{\frac{s_1^2}{n_1}+\frac{s_2^2}{n_2}}} \]
  • Use \(df=\min(n_1-1,n_2-1)\).
  • Need large samples or approximately normal data in both groups.
