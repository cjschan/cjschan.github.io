Section 5.1

Hypothesis Tests Using Normal Distributions

When the sampling distribution is approximately normal, we can replace the randomization distribution with a smooth curve and use z-scores.

Normal Distribution

The symmetric, bell-shaped curve that appears in nearly all sampling distributions.

Central Limit Theorem

  • For random samples with sufficiently large \(n\), the distribution of many common sample statistics is approximately normal.
  • The larger the sample size, the better the normal approximation.

Notation

\[ N(\text{mean}, \text{standard deviation}) \]
A normal distribution is fully described by its mean and standard deviation.

Using the Normal for Hypothesis Tests

If the randomization distribution is approximately normal, we can replace it with \(N(\text{null value}, SE)\).

From Simulation to Formula

  • Instead of simulating thousands of randomization samples, use the normal curve.
  • The p-value is the tail area beyond the observed statistic.

Example

Malaria and mosquitoes: statistic = \(-0.131\), null value = \(0\), \(SE = 0.056\)
\[ N(0,\ 0.056) \]
Compare the observed statistic to this distribution to find the p-value.

The Normal Curve in Action

A test statistic of \(z = -0.131 / 0.056 \approx -2.34\). Shaded tails show the two-tailed p-value.

The Standardized Test Statistic

Standardize to put every test on the same scale: \(N(0, 1)\).

z-Statistic

\[ z = \frac{\text{statistic} - \text{null value}}{SE} \]
This tells us how many standard errors the statistic is from the null value.

Example Calculation

\[ z = \frac{-0.131 - 0}{0.056} \approx -2.34 \]
The observed statistic is about 2.34 standard errors below the null value.

Finding the p-Value

The p-value is the tail area beyond the z-statistic in the standard normal distribution.

Process

  • Compute the z-statistic.
  • Find the area in the appropriate tail(s) of \(N(0,1)\).
  • For right-tailed: area to the right of \(z\).
  • For left-tailed: area to the left of \(z\).
  • For two-tailed: double the smaller tail.

Example

For \(z = -2.34\), left-tail:
\[ \text{p-value} \approx 0.0096 \]
Strong evidence that exposed mosquitoes are less likely to approach humans before becoming infectious.

Another Example: After Infectious

After the malaria parasite becomes infectious, do exposed mosquitoes approach humans more?

Data
Difference in proportions: \(0.151\), \(SE = 0.05\)
\[ z = \frac{0.151 - 0}{0.05} = 3.02 \]
Conclusion
\[ \text{p-value} \approx 0.0013 \]
Strong evidence that infected mosquitoes are more likely to approach humans after becoming infectious, favoring transmission of the parasite.

What to Remember

The normal distribution provides a shortcut for computing p-values.

Formula

\[ z = \frac{\text{statistic} - \text{null value}}{SE} \]
Works when the sampling distribution is approximately normal (CLT).

Process

  • Compute the z-statistic.
  • Find the p-value from \(N(0,1)\).
  • Compare the p-value to \(\alpha\) to make a decision.
