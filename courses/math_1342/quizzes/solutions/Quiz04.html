Quiz 4

Sampling Distributions and Bootstrap CIs

Worked Solutions

Covers Sections 3.1, 3.2, 3.3

Instructions. This is a take-home quiz. You may use your lecture notes and online resources, but the work you submit must be your own. Show your work clearly to receive credit.

Total: 10 points

Problem 1 [2 pts]

The dotplot below shows a simulated sampling distribution of the sample proportion \(\hat{p}\) of left-handed students, built from 500 random samples of size \(n = 100\). Read the reported statistics off the plot — do not use a formula.

  1. What is the center of this sampling distribution of \(\hat{p}\)? (0.5 pt)
  2. What is the standard error of \(\hat{p}\)? (0.5 pt)
  3. Using the reported mean and standard error, roughly what range of \(\hat{p}\) values would we expect? Use ±2 standard errors. (1 pt)

(a) The center is the reported mean of the sampling distribution, \(0.101\) (about \(0.10\)).

(b) The standard error reported on the plot is \(0.030\).

(c) Using \(\pm 2\) standard errors: \(0.101 \pm 2(0.030) = 0.101 \pm 0.060\), giving roughly \((0.041,\ 0.161)\).

Problem 2 [2 pts]

A 95% confidence interval for the mean nightly sleep time of college students is \((6.4, 7.2)\) hours. (1 pt each)

  1. Interpret this interval in plain language.
  2. True or false: there is a 95% chance the population mean lies between 6.4 and 7.2. Briefly explain.

(a) We are 95% confident that the true mean nightly sleep time for all college students in the population is between 6.4 and 7.2 hours. (The method used to build the interval captures the true mean in about 95% of all samples.)

(b) False. The population mean is a fixed (non-random) number — it is either in this particular interval or it isn’t. The 95% refers to the long-run success rate of the procedure across many samples, not the probability for this one fixed interval.

Problem 3 [2 pts]

Briefly describe how to construct one bootstrap sample from a sample of size \(n = 50\), and explain why bootstrapping is useful when we want a confidence interval but only have one sample. (1 pt each)

Constructing one bootstrap sample: Draw 50 observations from the original sample with replacement (each draw is equally likely to be any of the 50 original values, and values can repeat). This gives a new resample of size \(n = 50\), and we compute the statistic (e.g., the mean) on it.

Why it’s useful: Resampling with replacement many times simulates taking many samples from the population. The spread of the resulting bootstrap statistics estimates the standard error / sampling variability of the statistic, letting us build a confidence interval even though we only collected one real sample.

Problem 4 [2 pts]

A bootstrap distribution of the sample mean has standard error \(SE = 1.4\). The original sample mean is \(\bar{x} = 24.6\). Construct an approximate 95% confidence interval using the SE method (\(\bar{x} \pm 2 \, SE\)).

\(\bar{x} \pm 2\,SE = 24.6 \pm 2(1.4) = 24.6 \pm 2.8\).

This gives the interval \((24.6 - 2.8,\ 24.6 + 2.8) = (21.8,\ 27.4)\). We are about 95% confident the population mean lies between 21.8 and 27.4.

Problem 5 [2 pts]

A researcher takes a random sample of 60 college students and finds a sample mean study time of 14.2 hours per week. She constructs a 95% confidence interval and gets \((12.8,\ 15.6)\). Her colleague takes a different random sample of 200 students from the same population. (1 pt each)

  1. Would you expect the colleague’s 95% confidence interval to be wider, narrower, or the same width? Explain.
  2. Would you expect the colleague’s confidence interval to have the same center? Explain.

(a) Narrower. A larger sample size (\(n = 200\) vs. \(60\)) reduces the standard error (\(SE\) shrinks roughly like \(1/\sqrt{n}\)), and a smaller standard error produces a narrower confidence interval.

(b) Not necessarily the same center. A different random sample will produce a different sample mean, so the interval will be centered at the colleague’s \(\bar{x}\), which will likely differ somewhat from 14.2 (though both estimate the same population mean).
