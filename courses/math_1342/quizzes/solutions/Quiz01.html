Quiz 1

Data Structure, Sampling, and Experiments

Worked Solutions

Covers Sections 1.1, 1.2, 1.3

Instructions. This is a take-home quiz. You may use your lecture notes and online resources, but the work you submit must be your own. Show your work clearly to receive credit.

Total: 10 points

Problem 1 [2 pts]

For each variable, decide whether it is categorical or quantitative. (0.5 pt each)

  1. Eye color of a survey respondent
  2. Number of siblings
  3. Letter grade in a course
  4. Resting heart rate (bpm)

(a) Categorical — eye color names groups (brown, blue, etc.) with no numeric meaning.

(b) Quantitative — a count, so arithmetic such as averaging makes sense.

(c) Categorical — letter grades are labels (ordered categories), not measured numbers.

(d) Quantitative — a measured numerical value in beats per minute.

Problem 2 [2 pts]

A pollster asks every 50th visitor to a coffee shop their political party. Identify (a) the population, (b) the sample, and (c) one likely source of bias. (1 + 0.5 + 0.5)

(a) Population: all the individuals the pollster wants to draw conclusions about — e.g., all voters/adults in the community of interest, not just coffee-shop visitors.

(b) Sample: the visitors actually questioned — every 50th visitor to that coffee shop.

(c) Bias: selection/coverage bias — only coffee-shop visitors can be chosen, so the sample is not representative of the full population (it excludes people who never visit that shop and may over-represent certain ages or income levels).

Problem 3 [2 pts]

Match each scenario to the most appropriate sampling method (simple random, stratified, cluster, or convenience). (0.5 pt each)

  1. The instructor surveys the first 30 students who walk in.
  2. A list of all 800 employees is randomized; the first 60 are chosen.
  3. Voters are split into precincts; 5 precincts are chosen at random and every voter in those precincts is asked.
  4. Researchers separate hospital patients into <30, 30-59, 60+ years and randomly pick 20 from each age group.

(a) Convenience — whoever is easiest to reach (first to arrive) is sampled.

(b) Simple random sample — every employee is equally likely, and every possible group of 60 has an equal chance of being chosen.

(c) Cluster — the population is divided into groups (precincts), whole groups are randomly selected, and everyone in the chosen groups is surveyed.

(d) Stratified — the population is split into strata (age groups) and a random sample is taken from within each stratum.

Problem 4 [2 pts]

A researcher runs an experiment to test whether a new fertilizer increases tomato yield. Each plot is randomly assigned either the new fertilizer or the standard one. Identify the explanatory variable, the response variable, and what makes this an experiment (rather than an observational study). (0.5 + 0.5 + 1)

Explanatory variable: the type of fertilizer applied (new vs. standard).

Response variable: tomato yield.

Why it is an experiment: the researcher actively imposes a treatment by randomly assigning each plot to a fertilizer, rather than merely observing what plots already use. This random assignment of the treatment is the defining feature of an experiment and is what allows a cause-and-effect conclusion.

Problem 5 [2 pts]

A university wants to study whether living on campus affects first-year GPA. They compare GPAs of students who chose to live on campus with those who chose to live off campus. (1 pt each)

  1. Is this an experiment or an observational study? Explain.
  2. Can we conclude that living on campus causes higher GPAs? Why or why not?

(a) Observational study. The researchers do not assign students to live on or off campus — the students chose their own housing. No treatment is imposed; the groups are simply observed.

(b) No. Because students self-selected their housing (no random assignment), the groups may differ in other ways. Confounding variables such as family income, motivation, or commute time could explain any GPA difference. Without a randomized experiment we can establish only an association, not causation.
